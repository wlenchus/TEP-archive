# **1. Executive Summary**

The TEP budget equation x² + u = 1, identified with u = Dγ = 1 − γ²\_GMC/2, captures the geometry of quantum subsystem dynamics exactly for qubits and approximately for higher-dimensional systems. The spectral moment hierarchy provides the systematic corrections beyond the single-parameter (budget equation) description. The relationship between these frameworks is not one of competition but of **scale separation**: the budget equation governs the leading behavior, and the hierarchy governs the corrections.

The central structural result is the G-factor formula for the spectral dispersion F = p₃ − γ², which controls the purity speed limit. For qubits:

F = (x/√2)² / G²(√2·x)    where G(v) = 1/√(1 − v²)

This formula exhibits a **metric horizon** at x = 1/√2 (equivalently γ\_GMC = 1), where the generalized Lorentz factor diverges. For qubits, this horizon coincides with the maximally mixed state and is never crossed. For d ≥ 3, the system can cross this threshold, entering a **trans-critical regime** where the single-parameter description fails and the spectral hierarchy becomes essential. Horizons in this framework are thresholds beyond which a degree of freedom becomes dynamically relevant — a crossover rather than a phase transition.

# **2. What Is Structurally Established**

## **2.1 The G-Factor Formula (Qubits)**

For a qubit subsystem (d\_A = 2), the spectral dispersion F = p₃ − γ² is exactly determined by purity alone: F = (1 − γ)(2γ − 1)/2. In TEP budget variables (x² = 1 − γ):

F = (x²/2)(1 − 2x²) = (x/√2)² × (1 − 2x²)

The factor (1 − 2x²) = 1/G²(√2·x) is a Lorentz factor evaluated at the rescaled coherence √2·x. This is **exact**, following purely from the constraint that a qubit has one free eigenvalue. Verified numerically to machine precision. **\[SOLID\]**

## **2.2 The Metric Horizon at x = 1/√2**

The G-factor diverges at √2·x = 1, i.e., x = 1/√2 (γ = 1/2, maximally mixed qubit). At this point F = 0 and the purity speed limit |dγ/dt| ≤ 4‖V‖√F shuts off completely. For qubits, x\_max = 1/√2 exactly, so the horizon is never crossed. The dynamics approaches it asymptotically with critical slowing down. **\[SOLID\]**

This is precisely the “smoking gun” prediction from Section 4.2 of the Research Roadmap, now expressed in G-factor language. The horizon is structural (it follows from the eigenvalue constraint), not imposed.

## **2.3 Trans-Critical Regime Exists for d ≥ 3**

For d\_A = 3, x\_max = √(2/3) ≈ 0.8165, which exceeds 1/√2 ≈ 0.7071. The system can and does cross the qubit horizon. In this regime the G-factor formula gives F \< 0 (unphysical), but the actual F = p₃ − γ² remains positive because p₃ carries independent information. Numerically confirmed: the correction ε = F − F\_qubit is multi-valued as a function of x, proving it depends irreducibly on p₃. **\[SOLID\]**

## **2.4 Eigenvalue Activation as Horizon Mechanism**

The mechanism by which the system crosses the qubit horizon is the activation of the third eigenvalue λ₃. In the sub-critical regime, λ₃ is small and the system behaves as an effective qubit. As x → 1/√2, the two-eigenvalue description saturates. Beyond this point, λ₃ becomes dynamically significant and provides the additional degree of freedom that keeps F positive. **\[SOLID\]**

Crucially, this is a **crossover, not a phase transition**. λ₃ is nonzero from the start; it simply becomes *relevant* near the horizon. The eigenvalue activation thresholds (λ\_k/λ\_1 \> 1%) occur well before the theoretical horizon positions x = √(1 − 1/k). For d\_A = 5, the measured activation of λ₂ occurs at x ≈ 0.19, far below the horizon at x = 0.707.

# **3. Horizons as Relevance Thresholds**

The data supports a specific interpretation of horizons: they are thresholds beyond which a degree of freedom becomes (or ceases to be) dynamically relevant. This deserves careful unpacking.

## **3.1 The Precise Claim**

Each eigenvalue λ\_k of the reduced density matrix represents a dimension of the subsystem’s Hilbert space that is “occupied” by entanglement. When λ\_k is negligible, that dimension is dynamically inert — it contributes negligibly to the spectral moments and to F. The participation ratio PR = 1/γ = 1/p₂ counts the effective number of occupied dimensions.

The horizon at x = √(1 − 1/k) corresponds to PR = k. Below this horizon, fewer than k dimensions are effectively occupied, and the dynamics can be described by fewer than k independent spectral moments. Above it, k dimensions participate and k − 1 independent moments are needed.

The d\_A = 5 numerical data shows PR crossing 2 at t ≈ 2.0, coinciding with x ≈ 0.72 (just above 1/√2 = 0.707). At this point, the G-factor correction ε/F exceeds 1.0, meaning the qubit formula is no longer even qualitatively correct. The horizon marks where the effective dimensionality of the dynamics changes.

## **3.2 What This Means for TEP**

The budget equation x² + u = 1 with u = Dγ = 1 − γ²\_GMC/2 captures the dynamics when a single intermittency parameter γ\_GMC suffices — that is, when the entanglement spectrum is effectively one-dimensional (qubit-like). This is the **log-normal approximation** to the full multiplicative cascade.

The spectral moment hierarchy provides the systematic corrections: p₃ is the third cumulant (skewness), p₄ is the fourth (kurtosis), and so on. These encode the departure from log-normality — the non-Gaussian structure of the entanglement cascade.

The bridge statement: ***TEP’s budget equation is the leading-order (log-normal) description of entanglement dynamics. The spectral moment hierarchy provides the next-order corrections. The horizons mark the boundaries of validity for each truncation order.***

## **3.3 The Bidirectionality**

You asked whether horizons mark where a degree of freedom “becomes or ceases to be” relevant. The data confirms both directions. In the d\_A = 3 trajectory, the system crosses x = 1/√2 around t ≈ 2.0 (entering the trans-critical regime) and re-crosses around t ≈ 2.7 (returning to sub-critical). The participation ratio rises above 2, then falls below 2 again. The horizon is a dynamical boundary, not a one-way membrane.

This is physically sensible: unitary evolution is reversible. Entanglement can grow and shrink. When it shrinks, eigenvalues that were relevant become irrelevant again, and the simpler (budget equation) description regains validity.

# **4. The √2 Factors: Structure vs. Coincidence**

## **4.1 What the √2 Factors Mean**

The expression F = (x/√2)² / G²(√2·x) contains two √2 factors with distinct roles:

**Numerator: x/√2.** This normalizes the coherence x by the critical threshold 1/√2. It reaches unity at the horizon. In your framework’s language, the 1/√2 in the denominator signals a metric phase change — a threshold where an observable’s operational meaning changes. Here: below the threshold, purity dynamics is qubit-like; above it, new degrees of freedom enter.

**Denominator: G(√2·x).** The √2 rescaling of the G-factor argument means the dynamics “sees” a horizon at half the budget boundary. The purity dynamics has its own effective speed of light, distinct from the budget’s. The argument √2·x is precisely γ\_GMC, so G(γ\_GMC) = 1/√(1 − γ²\_GMC) is the natural Lorentz factor of the multiplicative chaos.

## **4.2 The Trans-Critical Interpretation**

Your suggestion of a “trans-critical measure” — a valid regime for which √2·x reaches beyond the threshold — is precisely what the data shows. For d ≥ 3, γ\_GMC can exceed 1, entering the regime where the log-normal (single-parameter) description breaks. The spectral hierarchy provides the regularization: F remains finite and positive because p₃ contributes independently.

The alternative reading — (1/√2)·x reaching beneath a threshold — describes the converse: regimes where the effective coherence is too small for a given degree of freedom to matter. Both are observed in the dynamics (crossing in both directions as entanglement oscillates).

What I cannot confirm from the numerics alone is whether the √2 factors carry a deeper structural load beyond the qubit case. For qubits, they arise algebraically from the eigenvalue constraint. For higher dimensions, the G-factor formula is replaced by F = p₃ − γ², which does not factorize into a product of a “normalized coherence” and a “Lorentz factor.” Whether there exists a higher-dimensional generalization with analogous factorization structure is an open question. **\[OPEN\]**

# **5. Critical Assessment of TEP Claims**

## **5.1 The Tautology Boundary**

The budget equation x² + u = 1 is, at its most basic, a tautology: any bounded variable can be written as the complement of its deficit. The question is whether the *identifications* (x² = γ²\_GMC/2, u = Dγ, etc.) carry physical content.

For qubits, the answer is: **barely**. The budget equation is the Pythagorean identity on the Bloch sphere. It constrains state-space geometry but not dynamics. The *dynamics* enters through the speed limit |dγ/dt| ≤ 4‖V‖√F, which involves the Hamiltonian and the state shape (via F). The budget equation tells you *where* you can be; the hierarchy tells you *how fast* you can move.

For higher dimensions, the budget equation becomes a genuine *approximation* — and therefore has falsifiable content. It claims that a single parameter (γ\_GMC) suffices. The hierarchy quantifies exactly when and how this fails. **\[SUBSTANTIVE\]**

## **5.2 The Hartmann Connection**

The Hartmann short-time result γ(t) ≈ 1 − (t/τ)² maps to the TEP structure as follows: u(t) = Dγ ≈ 1 − γ²\_GMC(t)/2, with γ\_GMC(t) ≈ √2 · t/τ at short times. This gives u ≈ 1 − t²/τ², recovering Hartmann. The subleading corrections depend on how γ\_GMC(t) deviates from linearity, which is where the spectral hierarchy enters.

The connection is **real but limited**: any quadratic initial behavior maps onto the budget structure at leading order. The subleading structure is where TEP must make specific predictions to avoid being merely a restatement of Taylor expansion. **\[REAL BUT NEEDS SHARPENING\]**

## **5.3 The GMC Dictionary**

The identification of spectral moments with GMC cumulants — p₂ ↔ γ²\_GMC (variance/intermittency), p₃ ↔ third cumulant (skewness), etc. — is structurally natural but not yet derived. The dimensional counting works: both sequences characterize the shape of a probability distribution (eigenvalues of ρ\_A for quantum, the multifractal measure for GMC). The correspondence F = 0 ⇔ log-normal cascade is clean.

What is missing is a proof that the mass exponent τ(q) of a specific GMC, evaluated at integer q, equals the spectral moments p\_q of a specific quantum state. Without this, the dictionary is an analogy rather than an isomorphism. **\[PROMISING ANALOGY, NOT YET PROVEN\]**

## **5.4 The Freezing Transition**

In standard GMC, the freezing transition occurs at γ\_GMC = 2 (in 1D), where Dγ → −1 and the measure becomes atomic. Under the direct identification u = Dγ = 1 − γ²\_GMC/2, the maximum γ\_GMC for a d\_A-dimensional system is √(2(1 − 1/d\_A)), which approaches √2 ≈ 1.414 as d → ∞. This is below the freezing point of 2.

This means either: (a) the freezing transition is inaccessible to finite-dimensional quantum systems and corresponds to a continuum/holographic limit; or (b) the identification needs modification for extreme entanglement. Both are interesting but neither is resolved. **\[OPEN\]**

# **6. How the Spectral Hierarchy Paper Connects**

The spectral moment hierarchy paper (on purity speed limits) and the TEP framework relate as follows:

## **6.1 The Hierarchy as Correction Series**

TEP’s budget equation with a single γ\_GMC is the zeroth-order description. It works when the entanglement spectrum is effectively two-dimensional (one free eigenvalue). The hierarchy’s results provide systematically higher-order corrections. Specifically, Result 3 (the tight qubit purity speed limit |dγ/dt| ≤ 4‖V‖√F with F depending on p₃) is the *first correction* beyond the budget equation for d ≥ 3.

## **6.2 F as Departure from Log-Normality**

The spectral dispersion F = p₃ − p₂² measures how much the eigenvalue distribution deviates from the shape predicted by purity alone. For qubits (log-normal cascade), F is a function of p₂ and carries no new information. For d ≥ 3, F is independent and measures the skewness of the entanglement spectrum — precisely the first non-Gaussian cumulant of the cascade.

The iso-purity prediction (states with same γ but different F have different speed limits) is the **experimental signature** of this departure. It cannot be stated or tested within the budget equation alone.

## **6.3 Publication Implications**

The hierarchy paper should cite the TEP framework as motivation/context *only if* the GMC dictionary can be made rigorous. Otherwise, the paper stands on its own mathematical merits (Cauchy-Schwarz hierarchy, tight bounds, Vandermonde reconstruction) without requiring the TEP interpretive layer. The connection to TEP is best presented as a discussion section: “Our hierarchy provides the correction terms to single-parameter descriptions of entanglement dynamics, analogous to how non-Gaussian cumulants correct log-normal cascade models.”
