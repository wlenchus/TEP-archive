### **3.1 The Cramér-Rao Derivation**

  

The Cramér-Rao bound is a fundamental result in statistics that sets a lower limit on the variance of any unbiased estimator of a parameter. If we are attempting to estimate a parameter $\\theta$ (such as temperature or position) from measurements of a system, the variance of our estimator $\\hat{\\theta}$ is bounded by the inverse of the Fisher Information $\\mathcal{I}(\\theta)$:

$$\\text{Var}(\\hat{\\theta}) \\ge \\frac{1}{\\mathcal{I}(\\theta)}$$

The framework reinterprets the total energy or capacity of a system as a budget of information. Let the total information capacity of a channel be $C$. This capacity is partitioned into "Captured Information" (that which allows estimation, analogous to Signal) and "Uncaptured Uncertainty" (that which contributes to variance, analogous to Noise).1

If we define $x^2$ as the normalized Fisher Information (the fraction of the total potential information that is actually captured by the measurement) and $u$ as the normalized residual uncertainty, we can write:

$$ x^2 = \\frac{\\mathcal{I}*{\\text{captured}}}{\\mathcal{I}*{\\text{max}}}, \\quad u = \\frac{\\mathcal{I}*{\\text{max}} - \\mathcal{I}*{\\text{captured}}}{\\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{max}}} $$

By construction, $x^2 + u = 1$. The physical assertion of the framework is that physical systems naturally evolve to saturation; that is, they occupy states that satisfy the Cramér-Rao equality. This "Efficiency Principle" suggests that the physics of the universe is optimized for information processing, or conversely, that physical laws are manifestations of the limits of information retrieval.3
