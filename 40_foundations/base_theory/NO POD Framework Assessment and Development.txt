### **2.2 The Mathematical Engine: Schur Complements and DtN Operators**

The mathematical validity of TEP rests on the properties of the Dirichlet-to-Neumann (DtN) map and the Schur complement. In the theory of electrical networks or discrete Laplacians, a system can be partitioned into boundary nodes ($b$) and interior nodes ($i$). $$\\begin{pmatrix} I\_b \\\\ 0 \\end{pmatrix} = \\begin{pmatrix} L\_{bb} & L\_{bi} \\\\ L\_{ib} & L\_{ii} \\end{pmatrix} \\begin{pmatrix} V\_b \\\\ V\_i \\end{pmatrix}$$

  

Here, $I\_b$ represents the flux (current) at the boundary, and $V\_b$ represents the potential (voltage). The interior nodes are assumed to have zero net flux ($I\_i = 0$), conserving energy internally. Solving for the interior potentials $V\_i$ and substituting back into the boundary equation yields the effective boundary operator:

  
  

$$L\_{\\text{eff}} = L\_{bb} - L\_{bi} L\_{ii}^{-1} L\_{ib}$$

  

This mathematical operation is the Schur complement.1 The critical insight provided in the framework is that this reduction operation preserves the fundamental physical properties of Passivity (positive-realness) and Reciprocity (symmetry).

If the original matrix $L$ describes a passive system (one that consumes or stores energy but does not generate it), the effective operator $L\_{\\text{eff}}$ also describes a passive system. This is non-trivial. It implies that however complex the internal topology—containing loops, cycles, and intricate connections—the boundary physics remains constrained by the laws of thermodynamics and causality. The "hologram" (the boundary operator) faithfully encodes the energetic constraints of the bulk.

In the frequency domain, this operator becomes a complex admittance function $Y(\\omega) = G(\\omega) + iB(\\omega)$. The framework identifies the physical meaning of these components within the TEP context:

$$\\text{Var}(\\hat{\\theta}) \\ge \\frac{1}{\\mathcal{I}(\\theta)}$$

$$x^2 \\equiv \\frac{\\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{captured}}}{\\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{max}}}$$

$$u \\equiv \\frac{\\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{max}} - \\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{captured}}}{\\mathcal{I}\_{\\text{max}}}$$

  

Thus, the equation $x^2 + u = 1$ is not a magical invocation but a tautology of optimal estimation. It describes a system that saturates the Cramér-Rao bound—an "efficient estimator."

This derivation has profound physical implications. It suggests that physical systems organize themselves to maximize information transfer (minimize $u$) or store information (maximize $u$) along an efficiency frontier. ### **3.2 Harmonic Analysis of GMC: The Rajchman Property**

To evaluate this hypothesis, we must examine the rigorous harmonic analysis of GMC provided in the research material. The paper by Garban and Vargas 1 focuses on the behavior of the Fourier coefficients of GMC on the circle. They prove a difficult and significant result: GMC measures are **Rajchman measures**.

A measure $\\mu$ is a Rajchman measure if its Fourier coefficients $\\hat{\\mu}(n)$ decay to zero as $|n| \\to \\infty$.

  
  

$$\\lim\_{|n| \\to \\infty} \\hat{\\mu}(n) = 0$$

  

This is not a trivial property. Singular measures (like the Dirac delta function or measures supported on certain Cantor sets) typically have Fourier coefficients that do not decay; they "ring" forever at specific frequencies. The fact that GMC—which is singular with respect to the Lebesgue measure and supported on a fractal set—possesses decaying Fourier coefficients implies a form of "asymptotic smoothness" or "high-frequency transparency."

Garban and Vargas establish that for sub-critical GMC (where the intermittency parameter $\\gamma \< \\sqrt{2}$), the measure dissolves into "white noise" at high frequencies but retains a specific fractal structure encoded in the rate of that decay. They provide bounds for the Fourier dimension of the measure 1:

  
  

$$\\frac{1}{2}\\left(1 - \\frac{\\gamma^2}{2}\\right) \\leq \\dim\_F(M\_\\gamma) \\leq 1 - \\frac{\\gamma^2}{2}$$

  

The Fourier dimension is a measure of the "capacity" of the support to suppress high frequencies. A higher Fourier dimension implies the measure is more "spread out" spectrally.

### **3.3 The Resolution of the Conjecture**

The subsequent paper by Lin, Qiu, and Tan 1 resolves the conjecture posed by Garban and Vargas. Using a powerful technique known as the vector-valued martingale method, they prove the exact Fourier dimension of 1D GMC:
