It replaces metaphysical speculation about a system's interior with an operational, testable theory of what can be known from the outside.

  

The framework is built on three core mathematical principles:

  

1\. The Schur Complement: TEP is based on the mathematical operation known as the Schur complement: L\_eff = L\_bb - L\_bi \* L\_ii⁻¹ \* L\_ib. This is a standard and powerful tool for systematically eliminating the interior variables (i) of a system of equations to find an effective law (L\_eff) that governs only the boundary variables (b). It allows us to "integrate out" the inaccessible interior of a system while preserving its complete external behavior.

2\. The Dirichlet-to-Neumann (DtN) Operator: The physical realization of the Schur complement is the Dirichlet-to-Neumann (DtN) map, denoted as Λ\_eff. This operator provides the effective law at the boundary. In practice, it is the function that transforms a potential applied to the boundary (a Dirichlet condition) into the resulting flux across that boundary (a Neumann condition). This operator contains all the information about the system's interior that is accessible from the exterior.

3\. The Positive-Real (PR) Condition: For any physical system that is passive (does not generate energy) and causal, the resulting boundary operator Λ\_eff must be a mathematical object known as a Positive-Real (PR) function. This is not a trivial constraint; it is a powerful mathematical condition that rigorously enforces the principles of causality and energy conservation on the boundary description. Any physically realizable system must produce a PR boundary operator.

  

Ultimately, the TEP framework is not a theory of substance but a theory of knowledge. It is a fundamentally epistemic principle that makes no claims about the ultimate nature of reality, only about the structure of the information we can access. "This is not a statement about what the universe is, but about what we can know." The first major test of this powerful, agnostic framework is its ability to derive the most familiar force in the universe: gravity.

3\. Kinematics: The thermal amplitude θ is directly coupled to acceleration a through the Unruh effect. The Unruh effect states that an accelerating observer perceives a thermal bath with temperature proportional to their acceleration (k\_B T ∝ a). In this framework, this implies a direct link between the thermal amplitude and acceleration: θ ∝ √a.

4\. Statistics: The average energy of thermal fluctuations is proportional to the total energy of a system multiplied by the variance: ⟨E⟩ ∝ E \* u.

  

Without any reference to Einstein's field equations, these four axioms lead directly to the conclusion that two masses will experience a correlated fluctuating force that reproduces the familiar form F ∝ G \* (m1\*m2)/r². The Equivalence Principle—the identity of inertial and gravitational mass—emerges as a natural consequence. Both inertia (the response to acceleration a) and gravitational weight (the emergent force) are sourced by the same underlying thermal amplitude θ, making their equivalence an inevitable outcome of the framework's structure.

This theoretical prediction has been validated by numerical simulations of quantum entanglement dynamics. These simulations investigate the evolution of a quantum system's purity (γ, which is equivalent to x²). The results confirm the critical nature of this 50% threshold: the rate of purity evolution (dγ/dt) goes to zero identically at γ = 0.5. This demonstrates that x² = 1/2 is not merely a mathematical curiosity but a true physical boundary that governs the dynamics of quantum systems.

Successfully deriving one of the most complex exact solutions in GR from purely informational constraints would serve as the "smoking gun" for the framework's validity in the domain of gravity and cosmology.

A specific experimental proposal, "Topological Acoustic Tomography," has been outlined.
