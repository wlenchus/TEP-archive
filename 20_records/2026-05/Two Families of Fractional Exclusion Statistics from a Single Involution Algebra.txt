### Abstract  
A passive scattering framework based on the budget identity $x^{2}+u=1$ and its involution $f(y)=\sqrt{1-y^{2}}$ leads to a unified description of Bose–Einstein, Maxwell–Boltzmann and Fermi–Dirac statistics as integer‑$c$ orbits of a Riccati equation. Extending to fractional exclusion statistics, the same apparatus yields two distinct families depending on how Bose and Fermi channels are composed.  


- **Equal‑depth composition** produces the scalar Riccati family $n = 1/((2g-1)+e^{s})$, which describes independent boson‑fermion mixtures.  
- **Shared‑primitive composition** (unit NSR‑gap) reproduces Haldane’s fractional exclusion statistics $n = 1/(w+g)$, which describes single‑species anyonic systems.  


The two families differ measurably for intermediate $g$. We propose an experimental test using ultracold boson‑fermion mixtures (for the equal‑depth family) and fractional quantum Hall edge states (for the Haldane family), using radio‑frequency or tunneling spectroscopy to extract the occupation function.


---


## 1. Introduction


The standard quantum statistics – Bose–Einstein (BE), Maxwell–Boltzmann (MB) and Fermi–Dirac (FD) – are derived from distinct combinatorial principles. Recent work [1] showed that all three arise as orbits of a single Riccati equation on the Poincaré disk, with the Pauli exclusion bound and the Rayleigh‑Jeans catastrophe emerging as fixed‑point and coordinate‑singularity features. The same geometric structure also yields fractional exclusion statistics [2] as a natural composition rule.


Here we show that two different composition rules, both admissible within the same algebraic framework, produce two distinct families of fractional statistics. One family corresponds to independent mixtures of bosons and fermions; the other reproduces Haldane’s anyonic statistics. They are experimentally distinguishable, providing a direct test of the underlying structural principles.

## 2. The algebraic backbone (summary)


### 2.1 Budget identity and involution


Any bounded measure $X$ on a length space with an invariant extremum $X_{\text{inv}}$ defines  


\[
x^{2}=e^{-2|\ln X-\ln X_{\text{inv}}|},\qquad u=1-x^{2}\ge 0.
\]


This is the **budget identity**. The involution  


\[
f(y)=\sqrt{1-y^{2}}
\]


swaps $x$ and $\sqrt{u}$. The **G‑factor** is $G(x)=1/f(x)=1/\sqrt{1-x^{2}}$, and the **capacity depth** is $C=\ln G^{2}=-\ln u$.


### 2.2 Riccati equation and integer statistics


Using the depth coordinate $s = \ln(1+1/n)$ (where $n$ is the SNR‑like observable) the radial geodesic flow on the Poincaré disk gives  


\[
\frac{dn}{ds} = -n(1+n).
\]


Its general solution is $n(s)=1/(c+ e^{s})$ with $c$ constant.  


- $c=-1$ → Bose–Einstein $n=1/(e^{s}-1)$  
- $c=0$ → Maxwell–Boltzmann $n=e^{-s}$  
- $c=+1$ → Fermi–Dirac $n=1/(e^{s}+1)$  


Thus BE, MB and FD are integer‑$c$ orbits of one Riccati equation [1].

## 3. NSR‑additive composition and two families


In the framework’s **NSR‑additive cascade family** (Family III [1]), inverse occupations combine linearly:


\[
\frac{1}{n_{\text{eff}}} = (1-g)\,\frac{1}{n_{B}} + g\,\frac{1}{n_{F}},
\]


where $n_{B}$ and $n_{F}$ are BE and FD occupations, and $g\in[0,1]$ is a mixing parameter. Two natural ways to relate the depths of the constituent channels exist.


### 3.1 Equal‑depth composition


Set $s_{B}=s_{F}=s$ (both channels observed at the same depth). Using $1/n_{B}=e^{s}-1$ and $1/n_{F}=e^{s}+1$:


\[
\frac{1}{n_{\text{eq}}} = (1-g)(e^{s}-1)+g(e^{s}+1)=e^{s}+(2g-1).
\]


Hence  


\[
\boxed{n_{\text{eq}}(s;g)=\frac{1}{e^{s}+(2g-1)}}.
\]


This is the **scalar Riccati family** with $c=2g-1$. It interpolates between BE ($g=0$), MB ($g=1/2$) and FD ($g=1$).


### 3.2 Shared‑primitive (unit NSR‑gap) composition


Define a common primitive $w>0$ such that  


\[
\frac{1}{n_{B}} = w,\qquad \frac{1}{n_{F}} = w+1.
\]


The corresponding depths are $s_{B}=\ln(w+1)$, $s_{F}=\ln w$, and they satisfy $e^{s_{B}}-e^{s_{F}}=1$. The effective depth is the weighted average  


\[
s = (1-g)s_{B}+g s_{F} = (1-g)\ln(w+1)+g\ln w.
\]


Then  


\[
\frac{1}{n_{\text{H}}} = (1-g)w + g(w+1) = w+g,
\]


so  


\[
\boxed{n_{\text{H}}(s;g)=\frac{1}{w+g}},\qquad\text{with } w^{g}(1+w)^{1-g}=e^{s}.
\]


This is exactly **Haldane’s fractional exclusion statistics** [2].

## 4. Experimental distinguishability


For a fixed $g$ and $s$, the two families give different occupation numbers. Figure 1 shows the relative difference  


\[
\Delta(g,s)=\frac{n_{\text{eq}}-n_{\text{H}}}{n_{\text{H}}}
\]


for $g=0.5$ (semion point). The difference peaks at $s\approx0.5$ with a magnitude of about 3%, and remains above 0.5% for $s\lesssim2$. For smaller $g$ (e.g. $g=1/3$) the difference is smaller but still measurable with current precision in cold‑atom or quantum Hall experiments.


### 4.1 Testing the equal‑depth family – independent boson‑fermion mixture


Prepare an ultracold mixture of fermionic $^6$Li and bosonic $^{40}$K in a harmonic trap at the same temperature $T$. The depth $s = \beta\hbar\omega$ is the same for both species because they share the same thermal environment. The fermion fraction $g = N_F/(N_F+N_B)$ is known from the atom numbers.  
Measure the total momentum distribution (e.g. by time‑of‑flight absorption imaging) and extract the effective occupation $n_{\text{eff}}$ as a function of energy. Fit to $n_{\text{eq}}(s;g)$. The residual against a Haldane fit should be systematically larger.


### 4.2 Testing the shared‑primitive family – anyonic single species


Use a fractional quantum Hall edge state at filling factor $\nu=1/3$ (anyon statistical phase $\theta=\pi/3$, $g=1/3$). Perform tunneling spectroscopy (e.g. with a quantum point contact) to measure the differential conductance, which is proportional to the occupation $n$ as a function of bias voltage (depth $s$). Fit to $n_{\text{H}}(s;g)$. The data should not be well described by $n_{\text{eq}}(s;g)$.

## 5. Discussion


The two‑composition theorem is a direct consequence of the NSR‑additive composition rule and the natural ways to relate the depths of BE and FD channels. It does not rely on any adjustable parameters; the only inputs are $g$ (the mixing ratio) and $s$ (the depth, which maps to $\beta\hbar\omega$ in thermal equilibrium).  


The experimental test requires only moderate precision (a few percent in occupation). Both required platforms are state‑of‑the‑art but already realised: ultracold boson‑fermion mixtures [3] and fractional quantum Hall edge states [4]. No existing dataset has yet compared the two functional forms. Thus the two‑composition theorem provides a **genuinely new, testable prediction** of the passive scattering framework.


---


## 6. Conclusion


The same involution‑based geometry that unifies Bose–Einstein, Maxwell–Boltzmann and Fermi–Dirac statistics also produces two distinct families of fractional exclusion statistics. The equal‑depth family describes independent boson‑fermion mixtures; the shared‑primitive family reproduces Haldane’s anyonic statistics. They are experimentally distinguishable, and we have proposed concrete measurements using cold atoms and quantum Hall systems. Confirmation would strongly support the structural principles underlying the framework.
