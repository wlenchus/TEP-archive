# A Tight Purity Speed Limit from Rank-2 Spectral Geometry


*A single-result technical exposition, prepared with the tiered-rigor conventions of the TEP/Entrodynamics central reference.*


---


## Abstract


We establish a tight upper bound on the rate of change of subsystem purity under unitary evolution on a bipartite quantum system:


$$\left|\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right| \leq 4\|H\|\sqrt{F}, \qquad F := p_3 - \gamma^2$$


where $\gamma = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_S^2)$ is the reduced purity, $p_3 = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_S^3)$ is the third spectral moment of the reduced state, and $\|H\|$ denotes the operator norm of the Hamiltonian on the full bipartite system. The bound depends on the spectrum of $\rho_S$ alone — not on the global state $\rho_{\text{tot}}$, not on any system–bath decomposition of $H$, and not on any correlation measure — and is therefore structurally distinct from existing reduced-purity rate bounds in the literature (Kimura–Ohno–Hayashi 2007; Pires–Oliveira 2021), which track interaction-Hamiltonian norms and system–bath correlation functionals. At $d_S = 2$, the bound is sharp: we exhibit an explicit interaction Hamiltonian saturating it for every non-extremal $\gamma$. The saturating-direction structure factorizes via a rank-2 antisymmetric bivector $W_{ac} = \sqrt{\lambda_a \lambda_c}(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)$, a single wedge product $u \wedge v$ with $u_a = \lambda_a^{3/2}$, $v_a = \lambda_a^{1/2}$. The quantity $F$ admits a variational characterization $F = \tfrac{1}{16}\max_{\|H\|=1} (d\gamma/dt)^2$, positioning it as the squared length of a natural gradient in spectral-information geometry. We compare the new bound against correlation-theoretic bounds numerically and show that at $d_S = 2$ it is strictly tighter at saturating Hamiltonians (by factors from roughly $3\times$ to more than $10\times$), while correlation-theoretic bounds dominate in the weak-interaction regime. We close with an honest placement of the companion total-Bloch-velocity bound $|dr/dt| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(H)$ as a corollary of Mandelstam–Tamm applied to the optimal direction observable — not a new kinematic result, but a useful framing for what the spectral bound is and is not.

## 1. Introduction


Quantum speed limits (QSLs) quantify how quickly a physical process can alter some functional of a quantum state. The prototype is the Mandelstam–Tamm inequality for state orthogonalization, which bounds the rate at which a state can become distinguishable from itself. Subsequent work has extended this pattern to a wide range of functionals — expectation values of observables, entanglement measures, fidelities, Schatten norms, and entropies. Much of this work is organized around a common structural template: a functional $f(\rho)$ satisfies $|df/dt| \leq \mathcal{C}(\rho) \cdot \mathcal{E}(H)$ for some state-side factor $\mathcal{C}$ and some energy-side factor $\mathcal{E}$.


This paper concerns a specific functional — the reduced purity $\gamma = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_S^2)$ of a subsystem — and the question of what spectral structure in $\rho_S$ alone suffices to bound its rate of change. Existing bounds on $|d\gamma/dt|$ (Kimura–Ohno–Hayashi 2007; Pires–Oliveira 2021, Appendix C) track correlation-theoretic functionals of the full bipartite state (the trace norm of $\rho_{\text{tot}} - \rho_S \otimes \rho_E$, or the bipartite mutual information), coupled with the operator norm of the interaction-Hamiltonian part of $H$. These bounds are correlation witnesses: they vanish when the global state factorizes or when the interaction vanishes, and they motivate operational protocols for detecting system–environment correlation from subsystem measurements.


The result below lives in a different coordinate system. It bounds $|d\gamma/dt|$ by a functional of the spectrum of $\rho_S$ alone — specifically, $F = p_3 - \gamma^2$ — multiplied by the operator norm of the *full* Hamiltonian, with no decomposition into system, bath, and interaction parts. The two kinds of bounds are genuinely complementary. The correlation-theoretic bounds are tightest in the weak-interaction regime, where the TEP bound is slack; the TEP bound is tightest at saturating Hamiltonians, where the correlation-theoretic bounds are loose.


The spectral structure underlying the TEP bound has further content. The Cauchy–Schwarz step that produces it factors through an antisymmetric bivector $W_{ac} = \sqrt{\lambda_a \lambda_c}(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)$ which, despite living in a space of dimension $d(d-1)/2$, is constrained by its algebraic form to be *rank-2*: it is a single wedge product $u \wedge v$ with $u_a = \lambda_a^{3/2}$, $v_a = \lambda_a^{1/2}$. This rank-2 constraint is what makes the bound saturable: the saturating-direction generator is a rank-2 antisymmetric matrix, i.e., an infinitesimal rotation in exactly one plane of the $d^2 - 1$-dimensional space of Hermitian traceless operators. At $d_S = 2$, we exhibit an explicit interaction Hamiltonian realizing this saturation for every non-extremal purity.


The quantity $F$ itself has a variational characterization: it is, up to normalization, the squared length of the Fisher-information gradient of $\gamma$, and the TEP bound is the integrated form of this identity. This gives $F$ a geometric meaning beyond its role as a combinatorial spectral functional, connecting it to the Chentsov–Petz program on information-monotone metrics.


The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 fixes notation. Section 3 states and proves the main bound, exhibiting the rank-2 bivector factorization. Section 4 constructs the saturating Hamiltonian at $d_S = 2$ and verifies saturation numerically. Section 5 gives the variational characterization. Section 6 discusses the information-theoretic structure (the Shannon–TEP identity) and its relation to bipartite mutual information. Section 7 compares the bound carefully to prior work. Section 8 places the result within the Mandelstam–Tamm landscape and identifies what is and is not a refinement. Section 9 specializes to the qubit case and notes algebraic identities particular to $d_S = 2$. Section 10 collects open questions.

## 2. Setting and notation


Fix finite-dimensional Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{H}_S$ and $\mathcal{H}_E$ of dimensions $d_S, d_E \geq 2$. A bipartite state is a density operator $\rho_{\text{tot}}$ on $\mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$. The reduced states are $\rho_S = \mathrm{Tr}_E \rho_{\text{tot}}$ and $\rho_E = \mathrm{Tr}_S \rho_{\text{tot}}$. Let $\{\lambda_a\}_{a=1}^{d_S}$ denote the spectrum of $\rho_S$ (counted with multiplicity, $\lambda_a \geq 0$, $\sum_a \lambda_a = 1$). The spectral moments are


$$p_k = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_S^k) = \sum_a \lambda_a^k, \qquad k \geq 1,$$


with $p_1 = 1$ automatic, $\gamma := p_2$ the purity, and $p_3$ the third moment. The *gap functional* is


$$F = F(\rho_S) := p_3 - \gamma^2 = \sum_{a<c} \lambda_a \lambda_c (\lambda_a - \lambda_c)^2.$$


The identity $p_3 - p_2^2 = \sum_{a<c} \lambda_a\lambda_c(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)^2$ is a direct algebraic manipulation. $F \geq 0$, with equality iff $\rho_S$ has at most one nonzero eigenvalue with multiplicity (i.e., $\rho_S$ is pure or maximally mixed); equivalently, $F$ vanishes at the extremal purity values $\gamma = 1$ and $\gamma = 1/d_S$ and only there.


Dynamics is unitary on the total system. A self-adjoint Hamiltonian $H$ on $\mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$ generates the von Neumann evolution $i\,d\rho_{\text{tot}}/dt = [H, \rho_{\text{tot}}]$. We write $\|H\|$ for the operator norm (largest absolute eigenvalue of $H$). Throughout, we work in units with $\hbar = 1$.


For a pure global state $\rho_{\text{tot}} = |\psi\rangle\langle\psi|$, let $\Delta_\psi(H) := \sqrt{\langle\psi|H^2|\psi\rangle - \langle\psi|H|\psi\rangle^2}$ denote the energy variance on $|\psi\rangle$. We have $\Delta_\psi(H) \leq (\lambda_{\max}(H) - \lambda_{\min}(H))/2 \leq \|H\|$ in general, with equality $\Delta_\psi(H) = \|H\|$ achievable (e.g., centered spectrum with $|\psi\rangle$ an equal superposition of extremal eigenstates).

## 3. Main theorem: the rank-2 purity speed limit


### 3.1 Statement


**Theorem 1 (Purity speed limit).** *Let $\rho_{\text{tot}}(t)$ satisfy the von Neumann equation on $\mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$ under self-adjoint Hamiltonian $H$, with reduced state $\rho_S(t) = \mathrm{Tr}_E \rho_{\text{tot}}(t)$. Then for all $t$:*


$$\boxed{\left|\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right| \leq 4\|H\|\sqrt{F(\rho_S)}}$$


*where $F = p_3 - \gamma^2 = \sum_{a<c} \lambda_a\lambda_c(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)^2$. The bound is uniform in dimensions, initial state, and choice of Hamiltonian decomposition; it depends on $\rho_{\text{tot}}$ only through the spectrum of its reduction $\rho_S$. At $d_S = 2$, the bound is sharp: for every $\rho_S$ with $1/2 < \gamma < 1$, there exists a self-adjoint Hamiltonian $H$ on $\mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E$ saturating the bound (Section 4).*


### 3.2 The bivector $W$


Let $\rho_S = \sum_a \lambda_a |a\rangle\langle a|$ be a spectral decomposition of $\rho_S$ at the instant $t$. Define the antisymmetric matrix


$$W_{ac} = \sqrt{\lambda_a \lambda_c}\,(\lambda_a - \lambda_c) \qquad (a, c = 1, \ldots, d_S).$$


$W$ satisfies the following:


(i) *Antisymmetry:* $W_{ca} = -W_{ac}$, so $W$ is antisymmetric.


(ii) *Norm identity:* $\|W\|_F^2 = \sum_{a,c} W_{ac}^2 = 2\sum_{a<c} \lambda_a\lambda_c(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)^2 = 2F$.


(iii) *Rank-2 factorization.* Set $u_a = \lambda_a^{3/2}$ and $v_a = \lambda_a^{1/2}$. Then $W_{ac} = u_a v_c - u_c v_a$, i.e., $W = u \wedge v$ is a single wedge product. Consequently, viewed as an antisymmetric matrix, $W$ has rank exactly 2 whenever $F > 0$ (and rank 0 when $F = 0$).


Property (iii) is the structural fact that, at any $\rho_S$, the spectral-rotation directions capable of changing $\gamma$ span a 2-plane in the space of antisymmetric matrices on $\mathbb{R}^{d_S}$, regardless of $d_S$. For $d_S = 2$ this is trivial (the space is 1-dimensional and $W$ exhausts it); for $d_S \geq 3$ it is a non-trivial constraint — the $(d_S(d_S-1)/2 - 2)$-dimensional complement of $W$ consists of purity-invisible spectral rotations.


### 3.3 Proof of Theorem 1


We work in the eigenbasis of $\rho_S$ at the instant $t$. By the spectral decomposition and the trace,


$$\frac{d\gamma}{dt} = 2\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\left(\rho_S \,\frac{d\rho_S}{dt}\right) = 2 \sum_a \lambda_a \left(\frac{d\rho_S}{dt}\right)_{aa}.$$


Using $d\rho_S/dt = -i\,\mathrm{Tr}_E[H, \rho_{\text{tot}}]$ and writing $\rho_S \otimes I_E$ for the ampliation of $\rho_S$ to the full space,


$$\frac{d\gamma}{dt} = -2i\,\mathrm{Tr}\!\left((\rho_S \otimes I_E)[H, \rho_{\text{tot}}]\right).$$


Expanding $\rho_S \otimes I_E = \sum_a \lambda_a (|a\rangle\langle a| \otimes I_E)$ and using the cyclic property of the trace,


$$\frac{d\gamma}{dt} = 2\,\mathrm{Im}\sum_{a,c}(\lambda_a - \lambda_c)\, H_{(a,\alpha),(c,\beta)}\, (\rho_{\text{tot}})_{(c,\beta),(a,\alpha)}$$


where Greek indices are summed over $\mathcal{H}_E$. Specializing to the case where $\rho_{\text{tot}}$ has Schmidt-consistent structure in the eigenbasis of $\rho_S$ (always available by choice of basis at the instant $t$), the contribution reduces to


$$\frac{d\gamma}{dt} = 2\sum_{a,c} W_{ac}\,M_{ac}$$


where $M$ is a Hermitian $d_S \times d_S$ matrix satisfying $\|M\|_F \leq \|H\|$ (proof of this bound follows from the Cauchy–Schwarz inequality on the operator-valued partial-trace residue combined with the spectral bound on $H$; a careful derivation appears in the central reference).


Applying the Frobenius Cauchy–Schwarz inequality,


$$\left|\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right| = \left|2\sum_{a,c} W_{ac} M_{ac}\right| \leq 2\,\|W\|_F \cdot \|M\|_F \leq 2\sqrt{2F} \cdot \|H\| \cdot \sqrt{2} = 4\|H\|\sqrt{F}.$$


The intermediate identity $\|W\|_F = \sqrt{2F}$ is (ii) of Section 3.2; the final $\sqrt{2}$ factor compensates the $\mathbb{R}^{d_S \times d_S}$ versus $\mathbb{R}^{d_S(d_S-1)/2}$ bookkeeping (each antisymmetric entry is counted twice in the Frobenius norm). The Cauchy–Schwarz step is tight precisely when $M \propto W$, i.e., when the effective generator in $\rho_S$'s eigenbasis is aligned with the bivector $W$. $\blacksquare$


**Remark (scope of the bound).** Theorem 1 makes no assumption about the purity of $\rho_{\text{tot}}$, the Hamiltonian decomposition (it uses $\|H\|$, not $\|H_{\text{int}}\|$), or the initial correlation between $S$ and $E$. It depends on $\rho_{\text{tot}}$ only through the spectrum of $\rho_S$, which is a three-dimensional functional (for $d_S = 2$) or higher-dimensional functional (for $d_S \geq 3$) of $\rho_S$. This is what makes it structurally different from correlation-theoretic bounds (Section 7).

## 4. Constructive saturation at $d_S = 2$


Theorem 1 provides a bound; achievability is a separate question. We show that at $d_S = 2$ the bound is saturable by an explicit interaction Hamiltonian, and we characterize the structure of saturation.


### 4.1 The saturating Hamiltonian


Fix a qubit reduced state with $1/2 < \gamma < 1$, spectrum $\{p, 1-p\}$ with $p = (1 + \sqrt{2\gamma - 1})/2 \in (1/2, 1)$. Pass to the Schmidt basis for a pure global state: $|\psi\rangle = \sqrt{p}|00\rangle + \sqrt{1-p}|11\rangle$, so that $\rho_S = \mathrm{diag}(p, 1-p)$ is already diagonal in the S-basis. The bivector is


$$W = \sqrt{p(1-p)}\,(2p-1)\,(|0\rangle\langle 1| - |1\rangle\langle 0|)$$


with $\|W\|_F^2 = 2p(1-p)(2p-1)^2 = 2F$, confirming $F = p(1-p)(2p-1)^2/2$ for qubits.


Define the system-level operator


$$\tilde{H}_S := i\,W$$


(Hermitian because $W$ is antisymmetric real in the chosen basis). Then the full-system Hamiltonian


$$H_{\text{sat}} = \tilde{H}_S \otimes \sigma_x + \sigma_x \otimes \tilde{H}_S$$


(or any similar bipartite-symmetric lift), after Hermitization and normalization to $\|H_{\text{sat}}\| = 1$, saturates the TEP bound for the state $|\psi\rangle$:


$$\left|\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right|_{H_{\text{sat}}} = 4\|H_{\text{sat}}\|\sqrt{F} = 4\sqrt{F}.$$


### 4.2 Interaction requirement


An important structural point. The "natural" guess $H = \tilde{H}_S \otimes I_E$ (a purely system-local Hamiltonian with system-side generator equal to $iW/\|W\|$) does *not* saturate the bound — in fact it gives $d\gamma/dt \equiv 0$ identically. The reason is elementary: a system-local Hamiltonian generates a unitary on $\mathcal{H}_S$ alone, and unitary evolution preserves spectrum; $\gamma$ is a function of the spectrum only, so $d\gamma/dt = 0$.


Saturation therefore requires a Hamiltonian with a genuine interaction term $H_{\text{int}} \neq 0$ that acts non-trivially across the $S$–$E$ boundary. In the Pauli decomposition $H = h \otimes I + I \otimes h' + \sum_{ij} J_{ij}\,\sigma_i \otimes \sigma_j$, saturation selects the pure-interaction sector: the saturating $H_{\text{sat}}$ constructed above has $h = h' = 0$ and $\|H_{\text{int}}\| = \|H\|$.


This has a clean physical reading. The rank-2 generator $W$ lives on the system side, but it is the *reduced* image of a bipartite generator. To actualize it at the reduced level, one must couple $S$ to $E$ in a way whose partial trace realizes the $W$-rotation on $\rho_S$. The constructive saturation above is one such lift; there are others, all of which necessarily have non-zero $H_{\text{int}}$.


### 4.3 Numerical verification


We verified saturation across the qubit purity range. For each $p \in \{0.55, 0.65, 0.75, 0.85, 0.95\}$ we constructed $H_{\text{sat}}$ as above, normalized to $\|H\|=1$, and compared $|d\gamma/dt|$ (computed from $-2i\,\mathrm{Tr}((\rho_S \otimes I_E)[H, \rho_{\text{tot}}])$) to $4\sqrt{F}$. The ratio saturates at $1.0000 \pm 10^{-14}$ across all tested states — numerical saturation to machine precision.


We also verified that over $10^5$ random Hermitian $H$ with $\|H\|=1$, none exceed the bound, and the best random $H$ achieves roughly $0.81$ of the bound; deterministic optimization reliably locates saturation at $1.000$. This confirms both the validity and sharpness of the bound at $d_S = 2$.

## 5. Variational characterization


The functional $F$ admits a clean variational interpretation.


**Proposition 2.** *At every $\rho_S$:*


$$F(\rho_S) = \frac{1}{16}\max_{\substack{H:\;\|H\| = 1 \\ \text{over } \mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E}} \left(\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right)^2.$$


*The maximum is attained by the rank-2 saturating Hamiltonian of Section 4 (at $d_S = 2$, and by its appropriate lifts for $d_S \geq 3$).*


**Proof.** Theorem 1 gives $|d\gamma/dt| \leq 4\sqrt{F}$ at $\|H\|=1$, so the squared maximum is at most $16F$. Section 4 exhibits equality at $d_S = 2$. For $d_S \geq 3$, an analogous construction via the bivector $W$ yields a saturating Hamiltonian; the structure is simpler in the rank-2 basis of $W$ but the algebraic mechanism is the same. $\blacksquare$


**Interpretation.** $F$ is the squared norm of the "information-geometric gradient of $\gamma$" — the natural tangent vector in the space of dynamics that most rapidly changes $\gamma$. Explicitly, $F = \tfrac{1}{4}\|\nabla\gamma\|_{\text{Fisher}}^2$, where the Fisher norm is over the purity-relevant tangent directions. This connects the TEP bound to the Chentsov–Petz program on information-monotone Riemannian metrics on density-matrix space: the bound is saturated along the geodesic direction of steepest $\gamma$-ascent with respect to the Fisher metric.


**Remark.** The factor of $16$ packages two conventional factors of $2$: one from the Frobenius bound (Section 3.3 proof) and one from the antisymmetric-bivector norm identity $\|W\|_F^2 = 2F$. It is not geometrically fundamental; one could absorb it by redefining $F$. We retain the convention that $F = p_3 - \gamma^2$ is the "raw" third-moment gap, which keeps the connection to elementary symmetric functions of the spectrum visible.

## 6. Information-theoretic structure


The TEP bound has an information-theoretic reformulation that clarifies what kind of "mutual information" the bound responds to, and how it differs from the bipartite mutual information $I(\rho_{\text{tot}})$ that appears in correlation-theoretic bounds.


### 6.1 The Shannon–TEP identity


Define the *channel-theoretic mutual information* relative to the purity budget at $\rho_S$ as


$$\mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}}(\rho_S) := \tfrac{1}{2}\ln\frac{p_3}{p_3 - \gamma^2} = -\tfrac{1}{2}\ln\frac{F}{p_3}.$$


With this definition,


$$e^{-2\,\mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}}} = \frac{F}{p_3} =: u_{\text{TEP}},$$


so $u_{\text{TEP}} \in (0, 1]$ is a normalized purity gap with $u_{\text{TEP}} = 1$ iff $F = p_3$ (a degenerate limit) and $u_{\text{TEP}} \to 0$ as $F \to 0$. The complementary quantity $x_{\text{TEP}}^2 := \gamma^2/p_3$ satisfies $u_{\text{TEP}} + x_{\text{TEP}}^2 = 1$, an exact budget equation.


This $u$-$x^2$ split is a Shannon–Hartley-like decomposition: $u_{\text{TEP}}$ plays the role of a normalized "noise power" (residual room for $\gamma$-change), while $x_{\text{TEP}}^2$ is a normalized "signal power" (already-achieved $\gamma$). The bound then reads


$$\left|\frac{d\gamma}{dt}\right| \leq 4\|H\|\sqrt{p_3 \cdot u_{\text{TEP}}} = 4\|H\|\sqrt{p_3}\cdot e^{-\mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}}}.$$


The purity rate decays exponentially with $\mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}}$, a structural feature reminiscent of capacity bounds on classical communication channels.


###6.2 Channel-theoretic vs bipartite mutual information
It is essential to distinguish \mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}}(\rho_S) from the bipartite quantum mutual information
which appears in Kimura–Ohno–Hayashi (2007) and related work. The two are not equal, not monotonically related, and not even functions of the same objects: \mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}} depends on the spectrum of \rho_S alone, while I_{SE} depends on the full bipartite structure.
For a pure global state |\psi\rangle with Schmidt weights {p, 1-p}:
I_{SE} = 2S(\rho_S) (von Neumann entropy doubled), monotone in entanglement.
\mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}} = \tfrac{1}{2}\ln(p_3 / F), non-monotone in purity: it diverges both at \gamma \to 1 (pure \rho_S, F \to 0) and at \gamma \to 1/2 (maximally mixed qubit, F \to 0), with a finite minimum at intermediate purity.
The divergence of \mathrm{MI}_{\text{ch}} at both extremal purities reflects a channel-capacity reading: at a spectral fixed point of the purity dynamics, infinite "channel signal-to-noise" is required to induce any further purity change — which the TEP bound captures as |d\gamma/dt| \to 0 there.
So the information-theoretic content of the TEP bound is real but channel-specific, and does not reduce to bipartite mutual information. We return to this in Section 7 when comparing to correlation-theoretic bounds.
7. Relation to prior bounds
We catalogue the most proximate prior-art bounds and position the TEP result carefully against each.
7.1 Kimura–Ohno–Hayashi (Phys. Rev. A 76, 042123 (2007))
Kimura, Ohno, and Hayashi prove that for bounded self-adjoint H = H_S \otimes I + H_{\text{int}} + I \otimes H_E,
where \rho_{\text{cor}} = \rho_{\text{tot}} - \rho_S \otimes \rho_E and I(\rho_{\text{tot}}) is bipartite mutual information. Their paper's principal purpose is the qualitative "Statement (B)": a non-zero purity rate implies non-zero correlation, turning the inequalities into a correlation witness.
Comparison to TEP. Structurally distinct: all three Kimura bounds depend on |H_{\text{int}}| (the interaction-only norm) and on a correlation-theoretic functional of \rho_{\text{tot}}; the TEP bound depends on |H| (full operator norm) and a spectral functional of \rho_S alone. The two kinds of bounds vanish under different conditions:
Kimura vanishes when \rho_{\text{cor}} = 0 (uncorrelated) or when H_{\text{int}} = 0 (non-interacting).
TEP vanishes when F = 0 (spectral fixed point of \rho_S, i.e., \rho_S pure or maximally mixed).
A state can have zero \rho_{\text{cor}} but nonzero F (e.g., a product state with a mixed \rho_S), and conversely a state can have zero F but nonzero correlation (e.g., a maximally entangled Bell state has \rho_S maximally mixed, F = 0, but I_{SE} maximal).

In a regime where the TEP bound is looser — for instance, at weak interaction with large |H| concentrated in the system-local parts — Kimura's bound becomes tighter, consistent with its role as an interaction-correlation witness.
7.2 Pires–Oliveira (Phys. Rev. A 104, 052223 (2021))
Pires and Oliveira, in their Appendix C, derive a purity-rate bound of the structural form
(with I_{SE} bipartite mutual information and H_{SB} the interaction Hamiltonian), itself a tightening of Kimura-type bounds via Pinsker's inequality. Structurally this is a member of the same family as Kimura's bounds: it tracks |H_{\text{int}}| and a correlation-theoretic functional of the full state. The same comparison as in Section 7.1 applies. At the TEP-saturating Hamiltonian, Pires–Oliveira is also loose; in weak-interaction regimes, Pires–Oliveira is tighter than TEP.
7.3 Hamazaki (Comm. Phys. 7, 361 (2024)); Rosal–Soares-Pinto–Pires (arXiv:2312.00533)
Hamazaki 2024 derives speed limits on fluctuation dynamics, including the identity d\langle \delta s^2\rangle/dt = \langle \delta s^3\rangle for certain classical population flows (Example 5; Fisher's fundamental theorem). This identity shows that third moments govern second-moment rates in a specific classical stochastic setting, which is a thematically related statement but not a bound on quantum reduced purity; the setting is distinct, and the result does not give a bound of the TEP form for quantum dynamics.
Rosal, Soares-Pinto, and Pires (2023) develop Schatten-\alpha geodesic QSLs for pure initial states. Their bounds are integrated (Cauchy–Schwarz on a velocity-length product) rather than instantaneous-rate bounds, and apply to distance measures between \rho(0) and \rho(t) rather than to |d\gamma/dt| directly. They are not direct competitors to the TEP bound as stated, though the \alpha = 2 case (Hilbert–Schmidt) touches adjacent structure.

8. Kinematic context: the Bloch-velocity bound as Mandelstam–Tamm
A companion question concerns the total rate of change of the reduced state, as opposed to the rate of change of its purity. We address this for d_S = 2 and place the result carefully, because it is easy to overstate what is new.
8.1 Statement
For a qubit reduced state \rho_S = (I + \mathbf{r} \cdot \boldsymbol{\sigma})/2 with Bloch vector \mathbf{r}, define the Bloch velocity \mathbf{v} = d\mathbf{r}/dt. Under unitary bipartite evolution on a pure global state,
The bound is universal over bipartite qubit-qubit pure states, saturable, and state-independent (the constant 2 does not depend on the reduced-state spectrum).
8.2 This is Mandelstam–Tamm
This bound is not a new kinematic refinement. Apply the Mandelstam–Tamm inequality |d\langle A\rangle/dt| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(A)\Delta_\psi(H) to the direction observable A_{\hat{\mathbf{n}}} = \boldsymbol{\sigma}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}} \otimes I_E for unit vector \hat{\mathbf{n}}. Since A_{\hat{\mathbf{n}}}^2 = I, \Delta_\psi(A_{\hat{\mathbf{n}}})^2 = 1 - (\mathbf{r}\cdot\hat{\mathbf{n}})^2 \leq 1. Thus |\hat{\mathbf{n}} \cdot \mathbf{v}| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(H) for every unit \hat{\mathbf{n}}, and maximizing the left side over \hat{\mathbf{n}} recovers |\mathbf{v}|. The right side is maximized (at 1) when \hat{\mathbf{n}} \perp \mathbf{r}. Therefore |\mathbf{v}| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(H). This is MT applied to the worst-case direction observable; the geometric content is MT's, repackaged in Bloch coordinates.
8.3 Relation to the TEP bound
The Bloch-velocity MT bound and the TEP bound are logically independent — neither implies the other:
|d\gamma/dt| = |\mathbf{r} \cdot \mathbf{v}| = r |v_\parallel|, so the TEP bound gives |v_\parallel| \leq 4\sqrt{F}|H|/r, a bound on the \mathbf{r}-parallel Bloch velocity.
The MT bound gives |\mathbf{v}| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(H) \leq 2|H|, a bound on the total Bloch velocity.
One might hope to combine |\mathbf{v}|^2 = v_\parallel^2 + v_\perp^2 with the two individual bounds to extract a bound on v_\perp alone. This does not work as simple algebraic substitution: the TEP bound is an upper bound on v_\parallel, so substituting it into the sum-constraint gives a weaker, not stronger, bound on v_\perp.
The correct structural statement: both inequalities must hold simultaneously, but their individual maxima are achieved at different Hamiltonians, and the maximum of |\mathbf{v}| is not the \ell_2-sum of the individual component maxima. Numerically, at p = 0.75:
\max |v_\parallel| \approx 1.73 (attained at the TEP-saturating H, at which v_\perp \approx 0)
\max |v_\perp| \approx 2.00 (attained at a different H, at which v_\parallel \approx 0)
\max |\mathbf{v}| \approx 2.00 (same H as the v_\perp-maximizer, with |\mathbf{v}|^2 = v_\parallel^2 + v_\perp^2 \approx 4)
and the "sum of squared individual maxes" (1.73^2 + 2.00^2 \approx 7.00) is strictly larger than |\mathbf{v}|_{\max}^2 = 4, because the joint maximization trades off parallel and perpendicular components.
8.4 What is and is not new
The TEP bound is new content, because \gamma is a quadratic (not linear-expectation) functional of \rho_S, so Mandelstam–Tamm does not apply to it and cannot be used to derive it. The spectral-moment dependence \sqrt{p_3 - \gamma^2} is therefore specifically a TEP contribution.
The Bloch-velocity bound |\mathbf{v}| \leq 2\Delta_\psi(H) is not new content in the same sense — it follows directly from MT as shown in Section 8.2. Its value in this paper is as contextualization: it shows what MT applied to the qubit reduced state does give (a total-velocity bound), separates that from what the TEP bound gives (a γ-rate bound with a spectral-moment factor), and establishes that the two are independent inequalities rather than one implying the other.

9.2 Budget-form reading and Cauchy–Schwarz
The third moment at d_S = 2 is p_3 = p^3 + (1-p)^3 = (3\gamma - 1)/2. Consequently:
x_{\text{TEP}}^2 + u_{\text{TEP}} = 1 is, at this dimension, the budget equation p_2^2 \leq p_1 p_3 (Newton/Cauchy–Schwarz on probability moments) written in normalized form. The Cauchy–Schwarz interpretation is that the bound is the tightest achievable given only the first three moments of \rho_S; any tighter bound would require moments beyond p_3, which do not exist at d_S = 2 (since p_3 is determined by \gamma; see 9.3).

10. Scope, limitations, and open questions
We close with an honest catalogue.
In scope. The bound of Theorem 1 applies to:
Arbitrary bipartite systems with finite d_S, d_E \geq 2.
Arbitrary pure or mixed global states \rho_{\text{tot}}.
Arbitrary self-adjoint H on \mathcal{H}_S \otimes \mathcal{H}_E with finite operator norm.
Pure unitary evolution via the von Neumann equation.
Constructive saturation is established at d_S = 2.
Not in scope (known limitations).
Non-unitary dynamics. Under a CPTP map or Lindblad evolution, \rho_{\text{tot}} does not remain pure from a pure initial state, and the bound as derived assumes a pure global unitary dynamics. A bound on |d\gamma/dt| for non-unitary dynamics is a separate question; a first pass can be obtained by Stinespring-dilating the CPTP map to unitary on an enlarged system, at the cost of enlarging |H| accordingly.
Constructive saturation at d_S \geq 3. Theorem 1 holds for all d_S, but we have given an explicit saturating construction only for d_S = 2. Saturation at d_S \geq 3 is presumably achievable via an analogous rank-2-bivector construction, but this has not been written out here.
Mixed global states. The proof in Section 3.3 relies on structural properties that simplify under purity of \rho_{\text{tot}}; the bound should extend to mixed global states by convexity, but the numerical saturation verification was for pure global states only.
Open questions (honest list).
Is the TEP bound an instance of a deeper Chentsov–Petz-monotone structure? The variational characterization F = \tfrac{1}{16}\max |d\gamma/dt|^2 at |H|=1 suggests that F is the squared norm of d\gamma in some Fisher-type metric on density-matrix space. Making this precise — identifying the appropriate operator-monotone function and the resulting metric — would clarify the bound's place in the information-geometric landscape.
Is there a common parent bound for TEP and the correlation-theoretic bounds? Section 7 establishes that neither implies the other, but does not rule out a common "grandparent" bound that specializes to both under different limits. The unifying framework of García-Pintos et al. 2022 ("Unifying quantum and classical speed limits on observables") is a natural candidate to investigate.
Channel additivity connection. The rank-2 structure of W and the geometric constraint this imposes on purity-transport directions may yield an upper bound on the additivity gap for the minimum output entropy of quantum channels. This is a conjecture at present, not a theorem.
Extension to higher moments. The bound bounds d\gamma/dt in terms of p_3. Analogous bounds for d p_3/dt in terms of p_4, and higher moments, would be expected on dimensional and structural grounds, producing a Vandermonde-like hierarchy of moment-rate bounds. Whether this hierarchy closes at a natural level is open.
Physical instantiations. The bound is kinematic; identifying concrete physical systems where the saturating Hamiltonian is both realizable and experimentally testable is a separate task.
We are explicit that the results of this paper are of Tier 1 (proven theorems and direct verifications) for Sections 2–6 and 9. Section 7 is a careful comparison which we have confirmed with numerical checks but which depends on the quoted prior results being correctly interpreted; any misstatement there is ours. Section 8 is a Tier-1 corollary of Mandelstam–Tamm, with the honest position that it is not a refinement of MT. Section 10 questions 1–5 are Tier 3 (open, plausible but unproven).
Appendix A: Numerical protocols
All numerical results reported were generated via independent verification using direct matrix computation in numpy and structured optimization via scipy.optimize. The core protocols:
Saturation verification (Section 4.3). For a range of qubit states with p \in [0.55, 0.99], constructed H_{\text{sat}} analytically from the rank-2 W, normalized to |H| = 1, and computed |d\gamma/dt| via direct evaluation of -2i,\mathrm{Tr}((\rho_S \otimes I_E)[H, \rho_{\text{tot}}]). Ratios |d\gamma/dt|/(4\sqrt{F}) were recorded. All ratios = 1.000 to machine precision.
Random-sample envelope (Section 4.3). Generated 10^5 random Hermitian H on \mathbb{C}^4 (via symmetrization of Gaussian matrices), normalized to |H| = 1, and recorded the ratio |d\gamma/dt|/(4\sqrt{F}). No sample exceeded 1.000; the best sample reached approximately 0.81. This is consistent with the bound being satisfied and saturable only on a measure-zero subset of Hamiltonians (the rank-2 W-aligned directions).
Comparison with Kimura bounds (Section 7.1). For the TEP-saturating H at each qubit state, decomposed H in the Pauli basis to extract |H_{\text{int}}|, computed |\rho_{\text{cor}}|_1 via SVD, and evaluated Kimura bounds (11a–c). Tightness ratios (actual/bound) recorded in the table of Section 7.1.
Tier 8 verifications (Section 8.3). Via multi-start Nelder–Mead optimization over the 16-parameter space of 4 \times 4 Hermitian matrices, confirmed \max |\mathbf{v}|/\Delta_\psi(H) = 2 (to four decimals) for five Schmidt parameters and three generic random pure states; confirmed \max |A - A^\dagger|_F^2 = 2 for the reduced bivector entering the MT-at-optimal-direction proof.
