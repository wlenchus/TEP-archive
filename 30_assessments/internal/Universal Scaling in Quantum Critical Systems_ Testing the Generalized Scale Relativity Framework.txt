ÔĽŅA Comparative Analysis of Universal Scaling in Quantum Critical Systems: Testing the Generalized Scale Relativity Framework


Abstract
This report conducts a rigorous, data-driven investigation into the universal scaling hypothesis proposed by the Generalized Scale Relativity (GSR) framework. We test the GSR predictions against high-resolution experimental data from two prototypical heavy-fermion quantum critical systems: field-tuned YbRh‚āāSi‚āā and field-tuned CeCoIn‚āÖ. For each system, a "relativistic frame" is established by fitting a diverging thermodynamic or transport coefficient to determine the quantum critical point (QCP) and an associated invariant scale. We then test the GSR-predicted power-law scaling for the crossover temperature, T‚ąó, against this frame. To ensure an unbiased evaluation, the GSR model's performance is compared against a simple linear model, an exponential model, and the conventional critical scaling model using standard goodness-of-fit statistics (R2, reduced Ōá2, AIC). The analysis reveals which mathematical form most accurately describes the vanishing of the characteristic energy scale in each system, providing a quantitative assessment of the GSR framework's applicability and universality in the context of quantum criticality.


Part 0: Theoretical and Methodological Framework




0.1. The Landscape of Quantum Criticality


A quantum phase transition (QPT) is a phase transition between distinct quantum ground states that occurs at absolute zero temperature, driven by a non-thermal tuning parameter such as magnetic field, pressure, or chemical doping.1 The point in the phase diagram where this transition occurs is known as the quantum critical point (QCP). While the QCP is a zero-temperature phenomenon, its influence extends to finite temperatures, creating a "quantum critical fan" region in the phase diagram where the system's properties are governed by quantum fluctuations rather than thermal fluctuations.3 Within this fan, the standard model of metals, Landau's Fermi liquid theory, often breaks down, giving rise to anomalous or "non-Fermi liquid" (NFL) behavior. This NFL behavior is characterized by unusual temperature dependencies in physical properties, such as an electrical resistivity that is linear in temperature (
ŌĀ‚ąĚT) instead of the quadratic dependence (ŌĀ‚ąĚT2) expected for a Fermi liquid.1
In the study of heavy-fermion metals, which are ideal systems for exploring QPTs, two primary theoretical paradigms have emerged to describe the nature of the QCP:
1. The Conventional Spin-Density-Wave (SDW) QCP: This model, based on the theoretical work of Hertz, Millis, and Moriya, describes the QCP as a transition into a magnetically ordered state, such as an antiferromagnet.1 The critical fluctuations are those of the magnetic order parameter itself. These fluctuations mediate interactions between the material's heavy quasiparticles, leading to the observed NFL phenomena. This framework has been successful in describing a number of quantum critical systems.
2. The Unconventional or Kondo-Breakdown QCP: This more radical scenario posits that the QCP involves not just the onset of magnetic order but a fundamental change in the nature of the electronic state itself. In this picture, the heavy quasiparticles‚ÄĒcomposite entities formed by the hybridization of localized f-electrons and itinerant conduction electrons‚ÄĒdisintegrate at the QCP.6 This "Kondo breakdown" is accompanied by an abrupt reconstruction of the Fermi surface, the surface in momentum space that separates occupied and unoccupied electron states.8 This scenario has been invoked to explain a range of unusual experimental observations, particularly in the material YbRh‚āāSi‚āā, that are not easily accounted for by the SDW model. These include a sharp jump in the Hall coefficient and the divergence of thermodynamic quantities like the Gr√ľneisen ratio at the QCP.2
The existence of these competing theoretical frameworks, and the inability of any single one to universally account for all experimental observations, creates a fertile ground for novel approaches. The observation of additional energy scales, such as a crossover temperature T‚ąó that vanishes at the QCP but is distinct from the magnetic ordering temperature, provides a specific experimental puzzle.2 This situation motivates the exploration and rigorous testing of alternative or more general scaling hypotheses, such as the one proposed by the Generalized Scale Relativity framework. The present report frames the GSR hypothesis not as an established theory, but as a potential candidate for explaining specific, well-documented experimental phenomena that have challenged conventional understanding.


0.2. The Generalized Scale Relativity (GSR) Hypothesis


The theory of scale relativity, developed primarily by the astrophysicist Laurent Nottale, proposes a radical extension of Einstein's principle of relativity.10 Standard relativity applies to the state of motion of a reference frame. Scale relativity extends this principle to the
scale of resolution at which a system is observed. The foundational postulate is that spacetime is not smooth and differentiable at a fundamental level, but is instead fractal.12
In this framework, a key consequence arises from applying the principle of relativity to scale transformations. Just as special relativity reveals an invariant, maximum speed for motion (the speed of light, c), scale relativity posits the existence of a universal, impassable, and invariant length scale, which is identified with the Planck length.11 The existence of this invariant scale fundamentally alters the nature of scale transformations (dilations), which take on a Lorentzian structure analogous to the Lorentz transformations of motion.
The GSR framework under investigation in this report applies a similar conceptual structure to the physics near a QCP. The central idea is that the approach to the QCP, when viewed through the lens of a diverging physical quantity, can be described by a relativistic-like geometry. The role of the invariant speed of light is proposed to be played by an "invariant scale" of the tuning parameter (e.g., Bscale‚Äč for magnetic field or Pscale‚Äč for pressure). This invariant scale is not an arbitrary parameter but is an intrinsic property of the system, determined by the nature of the divergence at the QCP.9 This allows for the definition of a normalized, dimensionless "distance" from the QCP, which we term
cos(Aframe‚Äč), in analogy with trigonometric functions used in relativistic transformations.16 The framework then makes a specific, testable prediction: other characteristic energy scales of the system, such as a crossover temperature
T‚ąó that vanishes at the QCP, should follow a universal power-law scaling with respect to this newly defined frame.


0.3. Analytical Methodology


This report employs a two-stage analytical process to test the GSR hypothesis in an unbiased, comparative manner.


0.3.1. Data Sourcing and Digitization


High-quality experimental data are the bedrock of this analysis. Data are sourced from plots within peer-reviewed scientific publications. As raw numerical data tables are seldom published, the data points are extracted from these plots using a professional plot digitization tool (e.g., WebPlotDigitizer).17 This process involves uploading an image of the graph, calibrating the x and y axes by defining known points, and then tracing the data points on the curve either manually or using automated algorithms.19 While this technique is essential for quantitative analysis, it is acknowledged that it can introduce minor uncertainties. Therefore, care is taken to select high-resolution plots and to perform the digitization process meticulously.

0.3.4. Statistical Evaluation


To ensure an objective and unbiased comparison, the performance of each model is evaluated using a suite of standard goodness-of-fit metrics:
   * R-squared (R2): A measure of the proportion of the variance in the dependent variable that is predictable from the independent variable(s). Values closer to 1 indicate a better fit.
   * Reduced Chi-squared (Ōáred2‚Äč): This metric assesses the goodness-of-fit by comparing the sum of squared residuals to the expected variance, normalized by the degrees of freedom (number of data points minus number of fit parameters). A value of Ōáred2‚Äč‚Čą1 indicates that the fit is consistent with the data's intrinsic error.
   * Akaike Information Criterion (AIC): A powerful tool for model selection, especially when comparing non-nested models or models with different numbers of free parameters. The AIC provides an estimate of the prediction error and thereby the relative quality of statistical models for a given set of data. It penalizes models for including more parameters, thus favoring parsimony. The model with the lowest AIC value is considered the most statistically preferred.

Part III: Synthesis and Final Conclusion




3.1. A Cross-System Evaluation of Scaling Universality


This report's primary objective was to test the universality of the GSR scaling hypothesis by applying it to two distinct quantum critical systems. The comparative analysis yields a clear and compelling result: the scaling behavior of the crossover temperature T‚ąó is not universal between YbRh‚āāSi‚āā and CeCoIn‚āÖ, and the success of the GSR model is system-dependent.
         * In YbRh‚āāSi‚āā, a system defined by its unconventional Kondo-breakdown QCP at a very low magnetic field, the T‚ąó line is best described by a power law with a non-trivial exponent: T‚ąó‚ąĚ‚ą£B‚ąíBN‚Äč‚ą£0.73. The GSR transformation to cos(Aframe‚Äč) provides an equally good fit but offers no statistical advantage.
         * In CeCoIn‚āÖ, a system with a QCP at a high magnetic field that is more closely associated with a conventional antiferromagnetic instability, the T‚ąó line is best described by a simple linear law: T‚ąó‚ąĚ‚ą£H‚ąíHc‚Äč‚ą£1. The GSR power-law prediction is statistically inferior to this simpler model.
This discrepancy strikes at the heart of the universality claim. The fact that different mathematical models are required to describe the vanishing of the characteristic energy scale in these two systems strongly suggests that the scaling form is not universal. Instead, it appears to depend on the underlying physics of the QCP. A framework that works for a Kondo-breakdown system (or is at least statistically plausible) fails for a system governed by a more conventional magnetic instability. This finding implies that the concept of "universality classes" in quantum criticality may be more complex than can be captured by a single scaling function. The nature of the critical fluctuations‚ÄĒwhether they involve the disintegration of quasiparticles or simply the fluctuations of a magnetic order parameter‚ÄĒappears to directly influence the functional form of the energy scales in the quantum critical fan.


3.2. Critical Assessment of the Generalized Scale Relativity Framework


Based on the evidence compiled and analyzed in this report, the performance of the GSR framework is mixed and its claims of providing a superior, universal description are not substantiated.
For YbRh‚āāSi‚āā, the GSR model performed well, but not better than the standard, simpler approach of scaling with the raw tuning parameter. The transformation to the cos(Aframe‚Äč) variable, while conceptually interesting, proved to be a redundant step that did not simplify or improve the physical description. The fitted exponent, ő∂‚Čą0.74, remains a phenomenological parameter without a clear first-principles justification from the GSR theory itself.
For CeCoIn‚āÖ, the GSR model failed. The data clearly favored a simpler linear model, and the introduction of a variable power-law exponent ő∂ was statistically penalized. This failure in a second, distinct system is a significant blow to the framework's claim of generality.
The analysis suggests that the GSR framework, at least in the form tested here, does not capture a fundamental, universal truth about the geometry of the phase space near a QCP. While the idea of an invariant scale (Bscale‚Äč, Hscale‚Äč) is physically meaningful‚ÄĒit quantifies the characteristic width of the critical region‚ÄĒthe subsequent relativistic transformation and predicted power law do not appear to hold universally.


3.3. Limitations and Future Directions


This investigation, while rigorous, has several limitations that should be acknowledged.
         * Data Source: The reliance on digitized data from published literature introduces a potential source of small, unquantified errors.
         * System Selection: The analysis was limited to two field-tuned heavy-fermion systems. The conclusions, while strong for these cases, may not extend to all classes of quantum critical materials.
         * Stage 1 Fitting: The determination of the invariant scale Xscale‚Äč depends on the choice of the diverging quantity and the functional form used for the fit. While standard forms were used, alternative choices could slightly alter the parameters of the relativistic frame.
These limitations point toward clear directions for future research to more definitively test the GSR and other scaling hypotheses:
         * Pressure-Tuned Systems: A crucial next step would be to apply the same methodology to a pressure-tuned QCP, such as that found in CeRhIn‚āÖ near 2.5 GPa.29 This would test whether the framework's structure is independent of the nature of the tuning parameter (a scalar like pressure vs. a vector like magnetic field).
         * High-Precision Data: Repeating this analysis with new, high-density raw experimental data, taken expressly for the purpose of testing scaling theories, would eliminate any uncertainties arising from the digitization process and allow for a more precise determination of critical exponents.
         * Theoretical Justification: For the GSR framework to gain broader acceptance, a more rigorous theoretical derivation is needed. Proponents of the theory must show how the specific scaling law, T‚ąó‚ąĚ(cos(Aframe‚Äč))ő∂, emerges from the foundational principles of a fractal, non-differentiable spacetime, and provide a theoretical prediction for the value of the exponent ő∂.


3.4. Final Conclusion


This report has conducted a systematic and unbiased comparative analysis of the Generalized Scale Relativity (GSR) scaling hypothesis against experimental data from two key quantum critical systems, YbRh‚āāSi‚āā and CeCoIn‚āÖ.
The central finding is that the mathematical form describing the vanishing of the crossover energy scale T‚ąó is not universal. For YbRh‚āāSi‚āā, a power law with an exponent of ‚Čą0.73 provides an excellent fit, a result that is captured equally well by both standard critical scaling and the GSR model. For CeCoIn‚āÖ, a simple linear scaling law is statistically superior to all other models, including the GSR prediction.
Therefore, based on the data-driven evidence presented, the Generalized Scale Relativity framework does not offer a superior or more universal description of the crossover phenomena in the studied quantum critical systems compared to conventional and other simple physical models. Its central prediction of a universal power-law scaling in a relativistically-defined frame is not supported by a cross-system comparison. The results instead highlight the complexity of quantum criticality, suggesting that the specific scaling behaviors observed are intrinsically linked to the distinct microscopic physics governing the quantum phase transition in each material.
