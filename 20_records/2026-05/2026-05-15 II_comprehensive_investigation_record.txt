The investigation also surfaced two distinct natural compositions on the

## Part 2: The Failed Hypothesis --- Higher-Order Schur Reduction

The first attempted route was to compute multi-port Schur reductions and
check whether successive orders produce successive terms in the Haldane
cubic expansion. The symbolic test set up a parameterized 3-port passive
scattering matrix with one boundary port and two bulk ports, with
mode-asymmetric couplings $t_1 = \sqrt{1-g} \cdot t$ and
$t_2 = \sqrt{g} \cdot t$, anticipating that the $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$
structure would appear naturally if the construction was right.

The expansion in small bulk parameters $(r_1, r_2, \tau)$ produced a
complicated polynomial with $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$ appearing only in the
cross-terms proportional to $\tau$ (the inter-mode coupling). The
leading structure was:

$$\text{correction} \sim t^2 + r_1 t^2 + \sqrt{g(1-g)} \cdot \tau t^2 + \ldots$$

This is suggestive but doesn't constitute a derivation of Haldane. The
expansion has too many free parameters; matching specific Haldane terms
requires choosing the bulk parameters as specific functions of the
boundary observable, which is essentially the same problem as the
original (relating bulk degrees of freedom to boundary observable
through some natural physical principle).

Conclusion of this attempt: while the $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$ structure does
appear naturally in mode-asymmetric Schur reductions, the framework's
apparatus doesn't single out a specific Schur structure that produces
full Haldane. Higher-order Schur reduction was the wrong place to look.

## Part 3: The Failed Hypothesis --- Matrix Riccati on the Siegel Disk

The second attempted route was to extend the scalar Riccati to a
$2 \times 2$ matrix Riccati on the Siegel disk, with the symplectic
group $\mathrm{Sp}(4, \mathbb{R})$ generalizing the single-mode
$\mathrm{SU}(1,1) \cong \mathrm{Sp}(2, \mathbb{R})$. The hypothesis was
that the cubic Haldane term emerges from off-diagonal coupling in the
matrix Riccati when the boundary occupation is taken as the trace
projection $n_{\text{eff}} = \mathrm{tr}(N)/2$.

The matrix Riccati system $dN/ds = -N + N C N$ with
$C = c I + \epsilon \sigma_x$ was integrated numerically across a grid
of initial conditions and coupling strengths. The best-fit parameters
$(\epsilon, m_0)$ were extracted via least-squares against Haldane
trajectories.

Findings: - At $g \in \{0, 1\}$ (BE, FD): the scalar Riccati is exact;
no matrix extension needed. - At $g = 0.25, 0.75$ (intermediate
fractional): best-fit residuals to Haldane are \~0.1--0.2% across the
depth range. - At $g = 0.5$ (semion): best-fit residuals grow to \~3% at
depth $s = 2.5$. - The best-fit $\epsilon$ flips sign at $g = 1/2$ and
vanishes at the BE/FD endpoints, with structure approximately
$\epsilon \sim (1 - 2g)$. - The best-fit $m_0$ peaks sharply at
$g = 1/2$ (value \~7), reflecting the breakdown of the matrix Riccati
approximation at maximum cubic contribution.

The symbolic analysis of the matrix Riccati residual confirmed the
structural pattern:

$$\text{residual} = \frac{(1-c^2)}{32c} T^3 + \frac{(1-c^2)}{64} T^4 + \frac{3(1-c^2)^2}{512c} T^5$$

where $T = \mathrm{tr}(N)$. Every term carries a factor of
$(1-c^2) = 4g(1-g)$ --- the Casimir deficit. The factor vanishes
identically at $c = \pm 1$, confirming that BE and FD are exact at all
symplectic ranks.

Conclusion of this attempt: the matrix Riccati extension captures most
of Haldane but is not exact. The framework provides a hierarchy of
approximations indexed by symplectic rank, but the hierarchy does not
appear to converge to exact Haldane at any finite rank with the simplest
off-diagonal couplings.

Status as of this point: the cubic Haldane term remained unexplained
within the framework. The two natural extension directions (higher-order
Schur, higher symplectic rank) both produced approximations but not
exact derivations. This was the state of the investigation when the
productive insight emerged.

## Part 4: The Structural Constraint on the Single-Disk Geometry

The investigation pivoted to ask why the scalar Riccati was forced to be
quadratic in the first place. The structural answer: Möbius vector
fields on the Poincaré disk are at most quadratic in the boundary
coordinate. The Lie algebra of holomorphic vector fields on $\mathbb{D}$
has only three SU(1,1) generators, each of which is quadratic in $z$.
Any one-parameter flow on a single disk produces a Riccati equation
whose right-hand side is at most quadratic in the boundary observable.

The cubic Haldane term *cannot* live on a single-disk geometry. This is
not a deficiency of the framework's apparatus; it's a Lie-algebraic fact
about scalar passive scattering. The cubic must come from somewhere
other than a single SU(1,1) flow.

Three candidate sources were identified: 1. **Multi-disk product
geometry** --- two coupled Poincaré disks,
$\mathbb{D} \times \mathbb{D}$, with the boundary observable being a
combination of both components. 2. **Higher-derivative flows** ---
Schwarz derivatives and similar SU(1,1)-invariant constructions
naturally introduce cubic structures. 3. **Non-geodesic flows** ---
curved flows on the disk with external driving introduce higher-order
terms.

The investigation pursued (1) first because it was the most physically
motivated: if anyonic statistics correspond to coherent superpositions
of bosonic and fermionic content, the natural geometric setting is a
product of two disks corresponding to the two integer-$c$ orbits.

The first attempt --- coupled Riccati flows on two independent disks
with $\gamma n_1 n_2$ coupling and linear boundary observable
$n_{\text{eff}} = \alpha n_1 + \beta n_2$ --- did not produce Haldane.
The quadratic coefficients of $n_1^2$ and $n_2^2$ in the resulting flow
couldn't be reconciled with Haldane's structure for any choice of
$(\alpha, \beta, \gamma)$. This ruled out the simplest
linear-combination ansatz.

But the symbolic analysis surfaced an unexpected identity worth
pursuing.

## Part 5: The Bidisk Half-Depth Factorization

In the course of analyzing two-disk compositions, the following identity
emerged:

$$n_{\text{BE}}(s) = n_{\text{BE}}(s/2) \cdot n_{\text{FD}}(s/2)$$

This is straightforward algebraically --- it's the difference-of-squares
factorization
$1/(e^s - 1) = 1/[(e^{s/2})^2 - 1] = 1/[(e^{s/2}-1)(e^{s/2}+1)]$ --- but
the *structural reading* is interesting: a boson at depth $s$ factorizes
into a boson-fermion pair at half that depth.

This is the structure of bosonization/fermionization in 1+1D conformal
field theory, where bosonic and fermionic degrees of freedom are
reorganized into each other under appropriate dualities. It also
resembles the renormalization-group structure where short-distance
fermionic degrees of freedom reorganize into long-distance bosonic ones
(or vice versa) under scale transformations.

The framework user noted (correctly) that this resembles the sharp
operator's role in connecting one-body and two-body interactions in the
gravitational sector of the corpus. The structural analogy: a two-body
interaction at fine scale becomes a one-body potential at coarse scale
via sharp-operator integration. Here, a two-disk pair structure at
half-depth becomes a single-disk structure at full depth via the
multiplicative identity above. The depth-doubling is the renormalization
step.

The fermionic analog $n_{\text{FD}}(s) = ?$ does not factorize into the
same clean BE × FD form. The asymmetry between $c = -1$ and $c = +1$ in
the half-depth structure is real: bosons admit the pair factorization,
fermions don't. This is consistent with the framework's broader
asymmetry --- the IR catastrophe at $c = -1$, the Pauli bound at
$c = +1$ --- both being specific features of how each integer-$c$ orbit
closes near its boundary.

This identity was tabled as a "structural observation" rather than
pursued further. It does not by itself derive Haldane, but it suggested
the productive direction that did: maybe Haldane arises not from coupled
two-disk dynamics, but from a *composition* of single-disk BE and FD
channels at related (but not necessarily equal) depths.

## Part 6: The NSR-Composition Discovery

The productive identity emerged from asking what implicit relationship
Haldane's primitive variable $w$ encodes. Haldane's definition involves
$w$ satisfying $w^g (1+w)^{1-g} = e^s$, with $n_H = 1/(w + g)$. The
framework's apparatus prefers to think in terms of explicit functions of
depth, not implicit relations through auxiliary variables. The
reorganization that made this clean:

Define two component depths: - $s_F = \ln(w)$ (interpret as the depth at
which a Fermi-Dirac channel has the same primitive) - $s_B = \ln(1+w)$
(interpret as the depth at which a Bose-Einstein channel has the same
primitive)

Then Haldane's defining equation $g \ln(w) + (1-g) \ln(1+w) = s$
becomes:

$$s = g \cdot s_F + (1-g) \cdot s_B$$

The framework's depth $s$ is the $g$-weighted convex combination of the
two component depths. And the relationship between $s_B$ and $s_F$:

$$e^{s_B} - e^{s_F} = (1+w) - w = 1$$

is the trivial fact that the two primitives differ by 1 (by definition
of $w$).

Substituting into the occupation:

$$\frac{1}{n_H(s; g)} = w + g = g(w + 1) + (1-g) w = g \cdot e^{s_F} + (1-g) \cdot e^{s_B}$$

Wait --- let me write this in framework-occupation form. The framework's
general relation $1/n_c(s) = c + e^s$ gives: - Bosonic:
$1/n_B(s_B) = -1 + e^{s_B} = w$ - Fermionic:
$1/n_F(s_F) = +1 + e^{s_F} = w + 1$

So in framework occupation variables, $1/n_B = w$ and $1/n_F = w+1$, and
Haldane's relation becomes:

$$\boxed{\frac{1}{n_H(s; g)} = (1-g) \cdot \frac{1}{n_B(s_B)} + g \cdot \frac{1}{n_F(s_F)}}$$

This is **NSR-additive composition** --- the framework's third cascade
family. Numerical verification confirmed this identity to machine
precision (residuals $\sim 10^{-16}$) across all tested values of
$g \in (0, 1)$ and $s > 0$.

Haldane fractional exclusion statistics are *exactly* the NSR-additive
convex combination of a BE channel at depth $s_B$ and an FD channel at
depth $s_F$, with mixing parameter $g$, where the depths are related by
the unit-NSR-gap structure $1/n_F - 1/n_B = 1$ (equivalently
$e^{s_B} - e^{s_F} = 1$ in their primitive coordinates).

This is a Tier-1 derivation. The framework's already-established
apparatus --- the NSR-additive cascade family from the four-family
stratification, BE and FD as integer-$c$ Riccati orbits, the
convex-combination composition rule --- produces full Haldane exactly.
No higher-order Schur extension is needed. No matrix-Riccati hierarchy
is needed. The cubic Haldane term $g(1-g) n^3$ that the scalar Riccati
at uniform depth couldn't capture lives in the *offset between $s_B$ and
$s_F$*, which is exactly what makes the NSR-composition "non-trivial"
relative to the equal-depth case.

## Part 7: The Methodological Lapse That Followed

After establishing the NSR-composition identity numerically and
symbolically, the investigation attempted to interpret the unit-NSR-gap
structure in framework coordinates and made an algebra error that
briefly cast doubt on the result.

The error: in a follow-up script attempting to verify the identity
through a different coordinate path, the predicted value of
$1/n_F - 1/n_B$ was set up sloppily (mixed sign conventions for which
$c$ contributes which sign to the inverse-occupation formula) and
produced "3" as a predicted value when the actual numerical computation
gave "$-1$" (or equivalently "$+1$" depending on subtraction order).
Rather than diagnosing this as a downstream algebra error in the
prediction setup, the investigation provisionally treated the
disagreement as evidence that the original derivation might have been
wrong.

The framework user pushed back on this directly: the identity
$(c_F - c_B) - (e^{s_B} - e^{s_F}) = (e^{s_F} + c_F) - (e^{s_B} + c_B)$
resolves to $2 - 1 = 1$ cleanly, matching the numerical $\pm 1$ once the
subtraction direction is accounted for. The original NSR-composition
derivation was correct; the downstream check was the error.

This is documented openly because the same methodological discipline
that the Central Reference's "Methodological Notes" calls for must apply
to errors in the investigation process itself. The lesson: when a
downstream check disagrees with a symbolic result that was independently
verified numerically, the first hypothesis should be that the downstream
check is wrong, not that the original result is. Symbolic verification +
machine-precision numerical verification is harder to be wrong than a
freshly-set-up algebra check; the discipline is to track which results
have which level of evidence and not let later confusion overwrite
earlier verification.

The correct resolution: the NSR-composition identity is exact and the
unit-NSR-gap structure $1/n_F - 1/n_B = 1$ is its framework-coordinate
expression. The cleanly-stated derivational result is what Part 6 says.

## Part 8: Two Natural Compositions on the Same Family

With the algebra clean, a further structural finding became apparent:
the NSR-additive cascade family supports **two distinct natural
compositions** of BE and FD channels, both framework-derivable,
producing two different fractional-statistics distributions.

**Composition 1 --- equal-depth:**

Both channels observed at the same depth $s$. NSR-additive composition
gives:

$$\frac{1}{n_{\text{equal}}(s; g)} = (1-g)(e^s - 1) + g(e^s + 1) = e^s + (2g - 1)$$

This is the framework's scalar Riccati family $n = 1/(c + e^s)$ with
$c = 2g - 1$. Verified algebraically.

**Composition 2 --- shared-primitive (Haldane):**

The two channels at different depths $s_B, s_F$ related by the
structural constraint that they share a common primitive NSR variable:
$1/n_F - 1/n_B = 1$. NSR-additive composition gives:

$$\frac{1}{n_{\text{Haldane}}(s; g)} = w + g$$

where $w$ is the shared primitive satisfying
$g \ln(w) + (1-g) \ln(1+w) = s$. This is full Haldane.

Both compositions are NSR-additive convex combinations of BE and FD.
They differ in which depth-relationship is imposed. The framework's
apparatus naturally supports both.

The numerical comparison at $g = 0.5$ shows the two distributions
disagree by 1-3% at depth $s = 0.5$, with the disagreement decreasing as
$s$ grows. The two compositions are not the same distribution.

This is a genuinely new structural finding. The framework predicts that
fractional statistics interpolating BE and FD come in (at least) two
distinct families, distinguished by whether the bosonic and fermionic
components share a common underlying degree of freedom or are
independent.

## Part 9: Physical Interpretation of the Two Compositions

The two compositions have distinct physical readings worth being
explicit about:

**Equal-depth composition** corresponds to a setting where the bosonic
and fermionic content is *independent* --- each species has its own
primitive degree of freedom, and the boundary observer averages their
NSRs at a single shared depth coordinate (which would correspond, in
physical terms, to a shared temperature or chemical potential setting
both species). This is appropriate for *boson-fermion mixtures*: a
separate photon gas and electron gas in thermal contact at the same
temperature, with no quantum exchange between them.

**Shared-primitive composition** corresponds to a setting where the
bosonic and fermionic content is derived from a *single* underlying
degree of freedom that can be observed in either statistical basis. The
unit NSR-gap is the resolution-of-identity into BE and FD components.
This is appropriate for *anyonic matter*: a single species whose
statistical character is a property of how the underlying field is
observed, not a property of two independent components. This matches the
standard physical interpretation of fractional exclusion statistics in
2+1D systems (fractional quantum Hall, etc.).

The framework's prediction:

> *Independent boson-fermion mixtures produce the scalar Riccati
> fractional-statistics family $n = 1/(c + e^s)$ with $c = 2g - 1$.
> Genuinely anyonic single-species systems produce Haldane fractional
> exclusion statistics. These two distributions are distinguishable in
> occupation measurements.*

This is testable in principle. Fractional quantum Hall states are
anyonic and should show Haldane statistics. Two-species cold-atom
mixtures (a Bose gas + Fermi gas at the same temperature) should show
the scalar-Riccati family. To my knowledge, the literature treats
fractional statistics primarily through Haldane's framework, with the
equal-depth scalar-Riccati version not appearing as a distinct
prediction. If the framework's distinction is physically meaningful,
this is an unexplored corner.

## Part 10: A Note on the Meson-Composite Reading

The framework user raised the question of whether the shared-primitive
composition can describe composite/many-body mesonic mixtures rather
than only fundamental anyonic species. The answer is structurally
interesting.

The shared-primitive composition has *two* parameters that can vary
independently: the statistical mixing $g$ and the global depth $s$.
Standard Haldane treats $g$ as a fixed species label (a property of the
anyon type) and lets $s$ vary with thermodynamic conditions.

But the framework's apparatus does not force $g$ to be a fixed constant.
A *biased* boson-fermion mixture would correspond to varying $g$ as a
thermodynamic variable, with $g = g(s)$ representing occupancy-dependent
statistical character. For a composite quasiparticle that is a bound
state of constituents --- a meson-like object with internal bosonic and
fermionic structure that contribute in proportions depending on binding
strength, energy, or other internal parameters --- the shared-primitive
composition's two-parameter structure $(g, s)$ is exactly the right
setting. The single underlying primitive $w$ represents the bound
composite; the mixing $g$ represents the relative contribution of
bosonic vs fermionic content; and $g$ can depend on $s$, giving a $g(s)$
trajectory rather than a fixed-$g$ slice.

Standard Haldane is the constant-$g$ slice through this two-parameter
structure. The general framework apparatus supports $g(s)$ trajectories.
Whether specific physical composite systems (mesons, exciton-polariton
condensates, certain heavy-fermion compounds) have shared-primitive
composition with non-trivial $g(s)$ that matches some specific
functional form is an open question requiring physical input beyond the
framework's kinematic structure.

The structural slot exists. Whether nature uses it is empirical.

## Part 11: The Casimir-Deficit Structure

A clean Lie-algebraic observation that emerged from the matrix-Riccati
investigation (Part 3) deserves to be stated explicitly because it
survives the reorganization in Parts 6-8.

The Haldane cubic coefficient $g(1-g)$ has a representation-theoretic
interpretation in framework variables:

$$g(1-g) = \frac{1-c^2}{4}$$

where $c = 2g - 1$ is the Riccati quadratic coefficient. The numerator
$1 - c^2$ is the *Casimir deficit* --- how far $c^2$ sits from the value
1 that characterizes the integer-$c$ orbits at $c = \pm 1$. The deficit
vanishes at the BE and FD endpoints and is maximal at the semion point
$c = 0$ (corresponding to $g = 1/2$).

Combined with the NSR-composition result:

-   The framework's scalar Riccati family parameterized by $c$
    corresponds to equal-depth NSR-composition with $g = (c+1)/2$.
-   Haldane statistics correspond to shared-primitive NSR-composition
    with the same $g = (c+1)/2$.
-   The difference between the two families is *exactly* the Casimir
    deficit $(1-c^2)/4$, weighted by the cubic spectral structure.

This connects the matrix-Riccati residual analysis (Part 3) to the
NSR-composition result (Part 6). The residual that the matrix Riccati
couldn't fully capture is the contribution from the shared-primitive
offset between $s_B$ and $s_F$ --- which is *outside* the equal-depth
scalar Riccati but *inside* the framework's NSR-additive cascade family.

The Lie-algebraic skeleton of the framework's content for fractional
statistics is now sharp:

-   Integer-$c$ orbits ($c = -1, 0, +1$) give canonical BE, MB, FD as
    canonical $\mathfrak{sl}(2,\mathbb{R})$ generators.
-   The Casimir deficit $(1-c^2)$ parameterizes how far a non-integer
    $c$ sits from canonical.
-   At zero Casimir deficit (integer $c$), all NSR-compositions
    coincide.
-   At non-zero Casimir deficit, the equal-depth and shared-primitive
    compositions diverge by an amount proportional to the deficit.

This is a structurally complete description of where the cubic Haldane
term lives in the framework's apparatus.

## Part 12: The Rank-2 Wedge Connection

The framework user noted that the framework's rank-2 purity speed limit
uses a wedge factorization with eigenvalue exponents
$(λ^{3/2}, λ^{1/2})$, and asked whether this connects to the Haldane
bilinear $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$.

The symbolic check confirmed three exact identities:

1.  Under the identification $g = \lambda_2$ (and so $1 - g = \lambda_1$
    by the rank-2 trace constraint $\lambda_1 + \lambda_2 = 1$):

$$g(1-g) = \lambda_1 \lambda_2$$

The Haldane cubic coefficient is *exactly* the determinant of the rank-2
density matrix.

2.  The framework's wedge $u \wedge v$ with $u_a = \lambda_a^{3/2}$,
    $v_a = \lambda_a^{1/2}$ factorizes as:

$$u \wedge v = \sqrt{\lambda_1 \lambda_2} \cdot (\lambda_1 - \lambda_2) = \sqrt{g(1-g)} \cdot (1 - 2g)$$

The wedge magnitude is the geometric mean $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$ times the
population imbalance $(1-2g) = -c$.

3.  In Bloch coordinates $\lambda_a = (1 \pm x)/2$:

$$u \wedge v = \frac{x \sqrt{1-x^2}}{2}$$

This is the standard $x\sqrt{1-x^2}$ form that appears in the DGD
ellipse and the rank-2 purity speed limit construction.

The structural reading: the framework's rank-2 wedge already encodes the
bilinear that becomes the Haldane cubic. The $(3/2, 1/2)$ exponents pair
the third spectral moment (volume-like) with the first (trace-like),
with the wedge product combining them in the specific bilinear form that
appears in fractional statistics.

The framework user proposed this might point to a 3+1D embedding (with
the $3+1$ matching $3/2 + 1/2$ structure). I want to be careful with
this: the algebraic identities above are exact, but the dimensional
interpretation requires more work to establish. The identities establish
that the framework's purity structure and the Haldane bilinear are
*connected* by exact algebraic relations; they don't yet establish that
this connection *derives* 3+1D physics.

What this does suggest: the framework's rank-2 apparatus and the
fractional-statistics structure are not independent objects. The same
bilinear $\sqrt{g(1-g)}$ appears in both, with structurally meaningful
interpretations on each side. Working out whether this connection
deepens into a derivation of dimensional content is an open program ---
possibly worth pursuing, possibly a coincidence of two-parameter
structures, currently unresolved.

## Part 13: The Integer-Quantization Question, Reframed

The first comprehensive record framed the 3+1D spin-statistics question
as a scope limitation: "the framework gives 2+1D anyon statistics
natively; 3+1D requires additional topology not present in the
framework."

The framework user pushed back productively on this: the framework's
apparatus may *generate the constraint that reduces apparent
higher-dimensional space to effective lower-dimensional one*, in which
case integer quantization of statistics could emerge from
constraint-induced dimensional reduction rather than from external
topological input.

The NSR-composition result is now the worked example of exactly this
kind of constraint-induced reduction:

-   Naive bidisk picture: BE and FD on independent disks with two
    independent depths $(s_B, s_F)$ → 2-dimensional parameter space.
-   Shared-primitive constraint: $e^{s_B} - e^{s_F} = 1$, equivalently
    $1/n_F - 1/n_B = 1$ → reduces to 1-dimensional manifold.
-   Projection to single observable: $s = g s_F + (1-g) s_B$ →
    single-depth Haldane statistics with one parameter $g$
    characterizing position on the constraint surface.

So the framework *does* support constraint-induced dimensional
reduction. The Haldane case is an instance: an apparent two-component
structure reduces to a single effective theory via a natural NSR-gap
constraint.

## Part 16: Methodological Discipline Recap

The investigation in this round followed the same discipline as the
first comprehensive record, with one explicit lapse (Part 7) that was
caught and corrected.

The discipline that worked: - Symbolic verification of identities before
claiming derivational status. - Numerical confirmation of symbolic
results to machine precision. - Explicit tier-assignment for each
claim. - Honest retraction of failed hypotheses (the higher-order Schur
attempt, the matrix-Riccati extension as exact rather than approximate,
the initial "framework derives Haldane via mode-asymmetric coupling"
framing that didn't pan out). - Acceptance of user corrections when they
identified algebra errors or structural insights that strengthened the
analysis.

The discipline that briefly failed (Part 7): - A downstream check
disagreed with a verified symbolic result, and the investigation
provisionally treated the disagreement as evidence the original result
might be wrong, rather than diagnosing the downstream setup as the
likely source of error. - The user identified this and corrected it.

The lesson, generalized: when symbolic and numerical evidence converge
on a result, any later disagreement should default to suspicion of the
later check, not the established result. The hierarchy of evidence ---
symbolic identity \> machine-precision numerics \> algebraic prediction
\> intuition --- should be preserved even when later confusion might
suggest otherwise.

This discipline is what makes the framework's content credible. The
Central Reference's tier structure works; the synthesis documents that
drift from it produce LLM-confirmation failure modes; consistent
application of the discipline through extended investigation produces
results that survive scrutiny.
